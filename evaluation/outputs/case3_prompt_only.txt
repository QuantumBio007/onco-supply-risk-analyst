# Drug Shortage Risk Brief: Cisplatin in Argentina
## Scenario: Active Pharmaceutical Ingredient (API) Export Restriction

---

## Drug Profile

**Drug:** Cisplatin (cis-diamminedichloroplatinum II)
**Therapeutic Class:** Platinum-based chemotherapy agent
**WHO Status:** Essential Medicine (cancer treatment)
**Argentine Formulations:** Injectable solution (10 mg/mL, 50 mL vials; 1 mg/mL bulk formulations)
**Primary Indications in Region:** Ovarian, testicular, bladder, lung, and gastric cancers
**Current Argentine Market Volume:** Estimated 8,000–12,000 treatment cycles annually across public/private systems
**Typical Shelf Life:** 24–36 months from manufacture

---

## Supply Chain Vulnerability

### Current Supply Structure

| Component | Source | Risk Level |
|-----------|--------|-----------|
| **API** | India (80%), China (15%), EU (5%) | **CRITICAL** |
| **Formulation/Packaging** | Argentina (Richet, local manufacturers), India | HIGH |
| **Distribution** | Regional wholesalers, ANMAT-licensed importers | MODERATE |

### Critical Vulnerabilities Under Export Restriction Scenario

**1. API Dependency Exposure**
- Argentina has **zero domestic API production capacity** for cisplatin
- 80% of regional API supply concentrated in 3–4 Indian manufacturers
- If India implements export restrictions (precedent: COVID-19 API hoarding 2020–2021), Argentine formulation capacity becomes orphaned within **6–8 weeks**
- China represents thin backup with geopolitical risk

**2. Buffer Stock Inadequacy**
- Most manufacturers maintain 4–6 week working inventory
- Argentine healthcare system (public PAMI, provinces) holds minimal strategic reserves
- Private sector hospitals typically stock 2–3 month supply for cost reasons

**3. Regulatory Bottlenecks**
- ANMAT approval for new suppliers averages **8–12 months**
- Emergency use pathways exist but require documentation from alternate suppliers (unavailable if restrictions global)
- Importation requires currency allocation—subject to Argentine forex constraints

**4. Market Concentration**
- Domestic formulation dominated by 2–3 manufacturers
- No inter-changeable generic competition reduces negotiating power with API suppliers
- Oncology drug import typically deprioritized in forex rationing vs. insulin, cardiovascular agents

## Scenario Impact Analysis

### Timeline & Progression

| Phase | Timeline | Impact |
|-------|----------|--------|
| **Phase 1: Announcement** | Week 0–2 | Panic buying; price volatility; informal hoarding by private sector |
| **Phase 2: Supply Squeeze** | Week 3–8 | Exhaustion of immediate stocks; price inflation 150–300%; rationing begins |
| **Phase 3: Acute Shortage** | Week 9–16 | Treatment delays/cancellations; diversion to private market; regional arbitrage |
| **Phase 4: Crisis** | Week 17+ | Systemic treatment disruption; mortality impact; regulatory emergency measures |

### Quantified Risk Estimates

**Treatment Access Impact:**
- Public sector (PAMI, provincial oncology centers): **60–70% of patients** experience 2–4 week treatment delays by month 2
- Private sector: **30–40% of patients** maintain access via direct importation attempts or alternative protocols
- Estimated **400–600 treatment cycles postponed/abandoned** over 6-month restriction period

**Price Escalation:**
- Current Argentine retail: ~$800–1,200 USD/patient cycle
- Projected under API restriction: $2,000–3,500 USD within 8–12 weeks
- Out-of-pocket affordability collapse for middle-income patients

**Mortality/Morbidity Proxy:**
- Delayed cisplatin initiation in testicular cancer: 5–10% relapse rate increase per 4-week delay
- Ovarian cancer: increased progression; reduced overall survival probability
- Estimated excess deaths (Argentina, 6-month period): **40–80 cases** (model dependent)

### Geographic/Socioeconomic Disparity
- **Buenos Aires private clinics:** Limited impact (direct import attempts, private supply chains)
- **Provincial public hospitals:** Severe impact (dependent on federal/provincial distribution; weaker negotiating power)
- **Rural/underserved regions:** Near-total access loss for 4–8 weeks

## Policy Recommendations

### Immediate Actions (0–4 weeks post-announcement)

1. **Emergency API Strategic Reserve**
   - Activate ANMAT/Ministry of Health to contract 8–12 week emergency API buffer
   - Secure allocation from neutral suppliers (EU, Switzerland if available)
   - Cost: ~$2–4M USD; justifiable under public health emergency

2. **Supplier Diversification Initiative**
   - Fast-track ANMAT pre-qualification of alternate API suppliers (Brazil, Mexico, EU)
   - Implement expedited documentation review (4-week pathway vs. standard 8–12 months)
   - Legal authority: ANMAT Emergency Use/Equivalent procedure

3. **Domestic Formulation Capacity Mapping**
   - Audit existing Argentine formulation capacity (Richet, others); identify surge capacity
   - Facilitate API stockpiling by licensed manufacturers with government guarantee of purchase
   - Estimated surge capacity: +30–40% within 6 weeks

4. **Price Regulation & Procurement**
   - Freeze retail/wholesale pricing at current levels (IOMA/PAMI authority)
   - Centralize procurement through Ministry of Health to consolidate demand (improve negotiating power with remaining suppliers)
   - Establish price caps: reject >120% markup justifications

### Medium-Term Actions (4–12 weeks)

5. **Regional Supply Coordination**
   - Engage MERCOSUR partners (Brazil, Uruguay, Paraguay) for API resource-sharing agreements
   - Brazil has some API capacity; formalize emergency supply pact
   - Regional purchase pooling to reduce individual vulnerability

6. **Alternative Treatment Pathways**
   - Convene oncology consensus panel to identify non-cisplatin protocols for select indications (e.g., carboplatin substitution with risk counseling)
   - Document in medical literature/guidelines to avoid off-label chaos
