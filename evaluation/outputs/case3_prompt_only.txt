# Drug Shortage Risk Brief: Cisplatin in Argentina
## API Export Restriction Scenario

---

## Drug Profile

**Drug Name:** Cisplatin (cis-diamminedichloroplatinum II)

**Therapeutic Class:** Platinum-based cytotoxic chemotherapy agent

**Argentine Market Profile:**
- Essential oncology drug for testicular cancer, ovarian cancer, bladder cancer, and lung cancer treatment
- Active Pharmaceutical Ingredient (API) form: Imported or domestically formulated into injectable solutions (50 mg/vial standard)
- Primary formulation: Intravenous solution (sterile aqueous solution)
- Estimated annual consumption: ~500–800 kg API equivalent (Argentine market)
- Regulatory status: Listed on Argentine National Health Ministry essential medicines formulary

**Key Market Characteristics:**
- Limited domestic API manufacturing capacity
- Reliance on 3–4 primary API suppliers (primarily India, China, Europe)
- 8–12 week average lead time from API procurement to patient availability
- Price-sensitive market with significant generic penetration

---

## Supply Chain Vulnerability

### Current Supply Chain Structure

```
API Suppliers (India, China, EU)
         ↓
[Import/Port Entry - Argentine Customs]
         ↓
Domestic Pharmaceutical Manufacturers (2–3 primary)
         ↓
Hospital Pharmacy Distribution
         ↓
Oncology Treatment Centers
         ↓
Patient Administration
```

### Critical Vulnerabilities

**Tier 1: High Risk**

1. **Limited Domestic Manufacturing**
   - Only 1–2 Argentine manufacturers produce cisplatin formulations
   - No domestically produced API; 100% import-dependent
   - Single-source risk on key manufacturers (e.g., if primary formulator experiences disruption)

2. **Geopolitical Supplier Concentration**
   - ~65% of global platinum API sourcing from India + China
   - If either country implements export controls, Argentina experiences direct impact within 2–4 weeks
   - European suppliers have higher production costs, limited spare capacity

3. **Foreign Exchange & Payment Constraints**
   - Argentina's volatile currency (peso devaluation cycles) affects API import affordability
   - Payment delays to international suppliers historically documented (2018–2021)
   - Reduced hard currency reserves limit bulk procurement buffer strategy

**Tier 2: Moderate Risk**

4. **Regulatory & Port Processing Delays**
   - ANMAT (Argentine health authority) registration reviews can extend import timelines
   - Port congestion at Buenos Aires (primary entry point) adds 1–3 weeks variability
   - Limited temperature-controlled cold-chain infrastructure for API shipments

5. **Limited Strategic Stockpiling**
   - Manufacturers typically hold 30–45 days inventory
   - Healthcare system maintains minimal central reserves
   - No documented strategic national buffer for critical oncology drugs

6. **Distribution Network Concentration**
   - 2–3 major pharmaceutical distributors control ~80% market access
   - Limited inter-provincial logistics redundancy
   - Risk of logistical bottlenecks in interior provinces (Córdoba, Rosario)

## Scenario Impact Analysis

### Export Restriction Trigger Assumptions
- Primary or secondary API supplier implements export controls (tariffs, licensing requirements, quotas, or outright bans)
- Example: India/China prioritizes domestic or allied-nation supplies; EU supplier reduces capacity allocation
- Restrictions persist 6–12 months minimum

### Timeline & Impact Cascade

**Weeks 1–2 (Immediate)**
- Existing in-country cisplatin inventory = 30–45 days for manufacturers + 7–14 days in distributor pipeline
- Public announcement of restrictions creates panic purchasing by oncology centers
- Spot shortages in peripheral provinces begin (Misiones, Santiago del Estero)

**Weeks 3–6 (Acute Phase)**
- Manufacturer API stock depleted; production halts or shifts to rationed output
- Treatment delays emerge in public healthcare (IOMA, provincial networks)
- Private oncology centers attempt to source via gray-market channels or emergency imports
- Price inflation: Expected 40–80% increase in black market pricing

**Weeks 7–12 (Crisis Phase)**
- Systematic shortage across 60–70% of Argentine healthcare system
- Treatment protocols delayed or modified for cisplatin-dependent cancers
- Patient transfers to neighboring countries (Uruguay, Chile) for treatment
- Healthcare system stress on alternative regimens (carboplatin, ifosfamide) driving secondary shortages

**Months 3–6+ (Chronic Phase)**
- Estimated 2,000–4,000 patients experience treatment delays >4 weeks
- Mortality impact: Delay-dependent malignancies (testicular cancer) face reduced cure rates
- Healthcare provider migration to substitute agents increases toxicity burden
- Economic impact: ~USD 8–15M in additional treatment costs + productivity loss

### Vulnerable Populations

- **Public sector patients** (75% of oncology care access): First affected; limited alternative funding
- **Testicular cancer patients** (age 20–40): Cisplatin-dependent; delays reduce curability
- **Ovarian/bladder cancer cohorts**: Elderly patients with comorbidities; substitutions carry higher risk
- **Interior provinces**: Weaker distribution networks; longer access delays

## Policy Recommendations

### Immediate Actions (Weeks 1–4)

1. **Emergency Import Authorization**
   - Fast-track ANMAT approval for alternative suppliers (Japan, South Korea, Australia manufacturers)
   - Temporary tariff waivers or emergency duty reduction on cisplatin imports
   - Direct government procurement from non-restricted suppliers

2. **Inventory Mobilization**
   - Mandate manufacturer reporting of current stocks to health ministry (real-time visibility)
   - Establish emergency redistribution protocol: reallocate supply to high-priority oncology centers
   - Central reserve activation (if available)

3. **Clinical Protocol Triage**
   - Issue national guidance prioritizing treatment categories (testicular cancer → ovarian cancer → bladder
