# Drug Shortage Risk Brief: Cisplatin in Argentina
## API Export Restriction Scenario

---

## Drug Profile

**Drug Name:** Cisplatin (Cis-diamminedichloroplatinum II)

**Therapeutic Class:** Alkylating agent; platinum-based cytotoxic chemotherapy

**Primary Oncology Indications in Argentina:**
- Testicular cancer (first-line)
- Ovarian cancer (standard component)
- Bladder cancer (muscle-invasive)
- Head and neck cancers
- Lung cancers (non-small cell and small cell)
- Gastric/esophageal malignancies

**Current Market Status in Argentina:**
- Essential Medicine (ANMAT-approved; covered by PAMI, OBRA social systems)
- Multiple generic formulations available
- Hospital-based distribution (primarily IV administration)
- Annual estimated consumption: 800–1,200 kg API equivalent
- Average retail price range: ARS 2,500–4,200 per 50mg vial (2024 estimates)

**Clinical Criticality:** HIGH — No direct therapeutic equivalents; dose deferral or omission compromises oncology treatment protocols

---

## Supply Chain Vulnerability

### Current Argentine Cisplatin Supply Structure

| **Supply Node** | **Dependency Level** | **Risk Notes** |
|---|---|---|
| **Domestic API Production** | Minimal (<5%) | Argentina lacks active platinum salt manufacturing; historical attempts (PLAPIQUI, state labs) discontinued post-2015 |
| **Import Sources** | Primary (>95%) | India (45–50%), China (30–35%), EU manufacturers (10–15%), minor: Brazil, Mexico |
| **Formulation/Packaging** | Mostly domestic | ~8 licensed manufacturers (Gador, Farmacia del Dr. Surtidor, Sidus, others); local fill-finish capacity = 60–70% of demand |
| **Distribution Network** | Public hospital procurement + private pharmacies | INCUCAI, provincial health systems, direct institutional contracts |

### Vulnerability Factors Under API Export Restriction

**1. Import Dependency Shock**
- **Scenario Trigger:** Exporting nations (India, China, or both) impose export quotas or licensing restrictions on platinum salts due to domestic shortage, geopolitical tension, or regulatory change
- **Argentine Exposure:** 95%+ of API sourced abroad; no strategic reserve mandated; typical inventory buffer = 2–4 months
- **Timeline to Impact:** 3–6 months post-restriction (accounting for in-transit stock and manufacturer reserves)

**2. Formulation Sector Fragility**
- Local manufacturers cannot synthesize API; dependent entirely on import agreements
- Small-to-medium enterprises lack long-term supply contracts with hedging provisions
- Currency volatility (ARS depreciation since 2022) inflates import costs; already compressed margins reduce business resilience

**3. Regulatory/Procurement Barriers**
- ANMAT import licensing can add 4–8 weeks if restrictions trigger additional scrutiny
- Provincial health budgets (co-financed federal/provincial systems) already strained; higher prices may delay institutional purchases
- No inter-provincial cisplatin pooling mechanism; unequal geographic access likely if supply becomes constrained

**4. Market Concentration Risk**
- Top 3 manufacturers control ~50% of Argentine cisplatin market
- If any supplier experiences API stock-out, redistribution is delayed by commercial contracts and regulatory approval of alternate sources

## Scenario Impact Analysis

### Impact Timeframe & Cascade

**Phase 1: Early Warning (Months 1–2 post-restriction announcement)**
- Export restrictions published by source country (India, China, or regulatory body)
- Spot prices spike 15–35% in global markets
- Argentine importers attempt bulk pre-purchases; some succeed, others face currency/credit constraints
- No visible shortage in pharmacies yet; healthcare facilities report no supply disruption

**Phase 2: Tightening (Months 3–6)**
- In-transit inventory depletes; new shipments delayed or reduced quantity
- Domestic manufacturers face API shortfall; production cuts of 20–40% announced
- ANMAT receives first shortage notifications from pharmaceutical companies
- Provincial hospital systems report allocation restrictions; elective oncology cases begin queuing
- Private oncology clinics (serving insured population) absorb supply first; public sector faces rationing

**Phase 3: Acute Shortage (Months 6–12)**
- Cisplatin availability drops to 40–60% of baseline demand
- Black market/unauthorized distributors emerge; counterfeit/substandard product risk ↑
- Treatment deferrals increase; median wait for chemotherapy cycles extends 2–4 weeks
- Socioeconomic disparities widen: insured/private patients have priority access

### Patient & System Consequences

**Oncology Service Impact:**
- Cisplatin-dependent regimens (PEB, BEP for testicular cancer; CAP for ovarian; CDDP/5-FU for head-neck) face protocol modifications or substitutions
- Multi-agent combination chemotherapy delays compromise efficacy windows in curative-intent settings
- Palliative care patient burden increases due to treatment deferrals

**Geographic Disparities:**
- Buenos Aires, Córdoba (major urban centers with private insurance penetration) experience less severe shortage
- Northwestern provinces (Misiones, Corrientes) with limited private oncology infrastructure face 50–70% access reduction
- Rural oncology centers may suspend cisplatin-based protocols entirely
