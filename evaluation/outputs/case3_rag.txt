## Drug Profile

Cisplatin is a platinum-based alkylating chemotherapy agent used to treat testicular, ovarian, bladder, lung, and head and neck cancers. It has been in clinical use since 1978 and is **included on the WHO Model List of Essential Medicines (oncology section) since 1979**, reflecting its status as a global procurement priority. The drug is fully off-patent with multiple generic manufacturers worldwide.

**Active Pharmaceutical Ingredient (API) Origin:** The overwhelming majority of cisplatin API is manufactured in India and China, which together account for over 80% of global cisplatin API supply. Argentina has no significant domestic API manufacturing capacity and is fully import-dependent.

**Generic Market Dynamics:** Price competition among generic manufacturers reduces profit margins and drives market consolidation, increasing vulnerability to supplier exits and manufacturing disruptions.

**Formulation & Storage:** Cisplatin is available as injectable solution or powder for reconstitution. Critically, it must be stored at room temperature (15–25°C) and protected from light; refrigeration causes precipitation and renders product unusable. This storage requirement differs from many other oncology injectables and is a relevant constraint for regional logistics.

## Supply Chain Vulnerability

**Concentration of API Supply:**
India and China supply over 80% of global cisplatin API. Any export restriction, regulatory action, or geopolitical disruption from either country simultaneously affects all generic manufacturers globally, eliminating substitution flexibility at the API level.

**Shared Platinum-Based Supply Chain:**
Cisplatin and carboplatin share the same API supply chain (both manufactured primarily in India, secondarily in China). An API export restriction disrupts **both platinum agents simultaneously**, compounding impact on oncology treatment programs that rely on platinum-based regimens.

**Argentina-Specific Regulatory Constraint:**
Argentina's ANMAT (Administración Nacional de Medicamentos, Alimentos y Tecnología Médica) must approve any new supplier or alternative manufacturer before legal distribution. Standard ANMAT registration timelines are 12–18 months for generic drugs. During an API export restriction scenario, this regulatory clearance requirement becomes a critical bottleneck: even if alternative suppliers exist globally, they cannot supply Argentina until ANMAT approval is complete. This regulatory lag **effectively extends practical lead time by 6–18 months** during supply disruptions.

**Structural Procurement Fragmentation:**
Argentina's multi-channel procurement landscape—public hospitals, obras sociales, PAMI, provincial drug banks, and private suppliers—creates significant visibility gaps. **No single entity has real-time operational visibility into national oncology drug stock across all channels.** Formal coverage through obras sociales or PAMI does not guarantee effective access; each step in the reimbursement and dispensing chain (clinical authorization, expediente processing, supplier contract, physical delivery) introduces latency and risk of failure. The gap between nominal coverage and effective access is a **structural feature of the Argentine health system**, not an exceptional failure mode.

**Historical Context:**
The 2023–2024 global cisplatin/carboplatin shortage, driven by Indian API manufacturing issues and US FDA-forced plant shutdowns, was superimposed on Argentina's domestic structural access constraints, creating compounded scarcity for two of the three most-used solid-tumor chemotherapy agents.

**Risk Assessment:**
Overall supply risk is **LOW**. Cisplatin supply in Argentina is projected to experience 7.9 stockout days per year under API export restriction, with a unit service level of 98.0%, which falls within acceptable operational range. However, the 30% probability of any stockout and the potential for isolated disruption events warrant continuous monitoring, particularly given the structural fragmentation in Argentina's procurement landscape.

**Interpretation:**
The modeled 50% fill rate assumption reflects severe supply allocation constraints documented during the 2023 cisplatin/carboplatin shortage. While the projected outcomes appear favorable, this analysis assumes that Argentina can access 50% of global cisplatin supply during the disruption period. In practice, allocation to Argentina would be subject to bilateral negotiations, international protocols, and competition from higher-income countries, making actual access outcomes highly uncertain.

## Policy Recommendations

1. **Establish a National Strategic Buffer Stock:**
   - Maintain 60–90 days of strategic cisplatin reserves at a centralized node (e.g., Ministry of Health warehouse or regional pharmaceutical hub) accessible to public hospitals, obras sociales, and provincial systems.
   - Conduct quarterly cycle counts to ensure stock quality and rotation compliance with room-temperature storage requirements.

2. **Create an Integrated National Visibility System:**
   - Develop a real-time operational inventory dashboard that aggregates cisplatin stock levels across public hospitals, obras sociales, PAMI, and provincial drug banks.
   - This system should enable emergency redistribution within 24–48 hours when localized shortages occur, bypassing formal procurement cycles.
   - Clarify data governance, security, and privacy protocols to ensure institutional participation.

3. **Streamline ANMAT Registration for Alternative Suppliers:**
   - Establish a fast-track or expedited pathway for ANMAT approval of new cisplatin suppliers during declared supply emergencies, reducing 12–18 month timelines to 4–8 weeks.
   - Pre-negotiate regulatory pre-qualification with 2–3 secondary suppliers outside India and China to reduce lead time should primary sources fail.

4. **Coordinate Cross-Channel Procurement:**
   - Convene quarterly meetings between ANMAT, public hospital procurement directors, obras sociales pharmacy committees, and PAMI to align demand forecasting and negotiate joint procurement contracts.
   - Establish transparent allocation protocols if supply is constrained, ensuring equitable access across insurance channels and geographic regions.

5. **Develop Regional Substitution Protocols:**
   - Although cisplatin and carboplatin share the same API supply chain and cannot substitute during simultaneous disruptions, clarify clinical protocols for alternate solid-tumor regimens (5-FU-based, taxane-based) that may be alternatives in non-cisplatin-dependent cancers.
   - Work with institutional oncology committees to pre-authorize protocol modifications when platinum agents are unavailable, reducing clinical delay during shortage events.

6. **Enhance Supply Chain Intelligence:**
   - Establish a formal mechanism for ANMAT and health ministry to monitor Indian and Chinese API manufacturing incidents, regulatory actions, and export policy signals in real time.
   - Create a monthly executive brief on global cisplatin and carboplatin supply status (API production, regulatory enforcement, geopolitical risks) circulated to hospital pharmacy leaders and payers.

7. **Address Structural Access Gaps:**
   - Audit the reimbursement and dispensing workflows that create latency between nominal obra social/PAMI coverage and actual patient access.
   - Set maximum timelines (e.g., 5 business days) for expediente processing and supplier payment for cisplatin and other essential oncology drugs, with financial penalties for delays.

## Confidence & Limitations

**Confidence in Projections:**
The Monte Carlo simulation results (7.9 stockout days, 98.0% service level) are based on transparent demand distributions, inventory policy optimization (safety stock, reorder point, EOQ), and stochastic lead time modeling. The mean disruption duration of ~90 days is grounded in published literature (Badejo & Ierapetritou, 2022; AIChE 2025) on supply chain shock recovery dynamics. These elements provide a reasonable operational framework for planning.

**Key Limitations:**

1. **Fill Rate Assumption Uncertainty:**
   The 50% effective order fill rate (structural floor 90% × scenario constraint 55%) is derived from 2023–2024 shortage observations but is illustrative, not deterministic. During an actual API export restriction, Argentina's allocation from global cisplatin supply would depend on bilateral trade negotiations, international protocols, and competition from higher-income countries — factors not modeled here. Real allocation could be lower (20–40%) or higher (60–80%) depending on geopolitical context.

2. **Procurement Fragmentation Not Fully Captured:**
   The simulation treats Argentina as a single inventory node. In reality, cisplatin stock is distributed across public hospitals, obras sociales regional warehouses, provincial drug banks, and private suppliers. The visibility gap means that 549 units of average inventory may not be geographically concentrated; localized stockouts in rural provinces or understaffed public hospitals could occur even when national aggregate stock is adequate. The 7.9 stockout days figure masks this heterogeneous risk.

3. **ANMAT Regulatory Lag Modeled as Fixed Lead Time:**
   The 12–18 month ANMAT registration requirement is a real institutional constraint, but it is represented in the simulation as increased lead time variability rather than as a discrete approval gate. If an API export restriction triggers a regulatory emergency, ANMAT's capacity to conduct accelerated reviews is unknown and not modeled. The actual time to approve an alternative supplier could extend beyond 18 months if dossier quality is poor or ANMAT capacity is constrained.

4. **Budget Constraint (75% multiplier) is Structural, Not Scenario-Specific:**
   Argentina's effective budget constraint reflects chronic fiscal austerity independent of the API export scenario. This means the model conflates baseline vulnerability with scenario impact. The "50% fill rate" already absorbs budget limitations; adding an API export restriction may not linearly multiply effects if Argentine health expenditure is already at a binding constraint.
