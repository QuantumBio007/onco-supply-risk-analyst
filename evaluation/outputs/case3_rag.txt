## Drug Profile

Cisplatin is a platinum-based alkylating chemotherapy agent used to treat testicular, ovarian, bladder, lung, and head and neck cancers. It has been on the **WHO Model List of Essential Medicines since 1979** and remains on the current oncology section. The drug is fully off-patent with multiple generic manufacturers worldwide. 

**API manufacturing is concentrated in India and China**, which together account for over 80% of global cisplatin API supply. **Argentina has no domestic API manufacturing capacity and is fully import-dependent**. Cisplatin is available as injectable solution or powder for reconstitution and must be stored at room temperature (15–25°C), protected from light. Critically, **cisplatin must NOT be refrigerated**, as refrigeration causes precipitation and renders the product unusable—a storage requirement different from many other oncology injectables.

---

## Supply Chain Vulnerability

**API Supply Concentration**  
India and China together control over 80% of global cisplatin API production. Any export restriction, regulatory action, or geopolitical disruption in either country simultaneously affects all generic manufacturers globally, including those supplying Argentina.

**Shared Platinum Supply Chain**  
Cisplatin and carboplatin—two of the three most-used solid-tumor chemotherapy agents in Argentina—**share the same API supply chain** (India and China as primary sources). An API export restriction disrupts both drugs simultaneously, eliminating substitution flexibility for oncology programs.

**Generic Market Margin Erosion**  
Price competition among multiple generic manufacturers reduces profit margins, driving market consolidation and leading some producers to exit the market entirely—further concentrating supply among fewer suppliers.

**Regulatory Lead Time Bottleneck**  
In Argentina, **ANMAT (Administración Nacional de Medicamentos, Alimentos y Tecnología Médica) must approve any new supplier or alternative manufacturer before legal distribution**. Standard ANMAT registration timelines are **12–18 months for generic drugs**. During an API export restriction scenario, this regulatory clearance requirement becomes a critical constraint—even if alternative suppliers exist globally, they cannot supply Argentina until ANMAT registration is complete. This regulatory lag effectively extends practical lead time by an additional **6–18 months** during supply disruptions.

**Structural Visibility and Access Gaps**  
No single entity in Argentina has complete, integrated, real-time operational visibility into national oncology drug stock across all channels (public hospitals, obras sociales, PAMI, prepagas, provincial systems). Although regulatory traceability systems exist, they are not equivalent to a national inventory dashboard. **The gap between formal coverage and effective access is a structural feature of the Argentine health system.** Many oncology treatments are nominally covered, but actual patient access depends on a downstream chain: clinical authorization, reimbursement file processing, supplier contract, dispensing logistics, and physical delivery. Each step introduces latency and independent risk of failure.

**Fiscal Constraint Cap**  
Argentina operates under a structural budget multiplier of **75%** due to chronic fiscal constraints (oil revenue collapse, structural austerity), independent of any scenario shock. This limits purchasing power for alternative suppliers during disruptions.

**Risk Assessment: LOW to MODERATE**

Under an API export restriction scenario, cisplatin supply in Argentina is projected to experience **7.9 stockout days per year** with a unit service level of **98.0%**. This falls within acceptable operational range for routine operations. However, the **30% probability of any stockout during the year** and the **47.3-day worst-case scenario** (CVaR₉₀) warrant active monitoring and buffer stock strategies.

**Interpretation**

The relatively favorable outcome (compared to Colombia's 11.9 stockout days and Venezuela's 30.9 stockout days) reflects Argentina's:
- Shorter disruption lead time (87.5 days vs. Colombia's 70 days at 46% fill, vs. Venezuela's 150 days at 33% fill)
- Higher effective fill rate (50% vs. Colombia's 46% and Venezuela's 33%)
- Better baseline procurement infrastructure and regulatory processes (despite ANMAT's 12–18 month registration lag)

However, this projection assumes that:
1. **ANMAT can expedite alternative supplier registration** during crisis (not guaranteed)
2. **Budget constraints do not tighten further** beyond the structural 75% cap
3. **Inter-hospital loan mechanisms and provincial drug banks** function as designed
4. **Informal clinician-led logistics** continue to resolve individual cases without system-level collapse

## Policy Recommendations

**1. Establish a National Strategic Reserve for Cisplatin (and Carboplatin)**  
- Create a dedicated **60–90 day buffer stock** at a central repository (e.g., BNDE or a designated provincial hub)  
- Stock level: minimum 250–300 units of cisplatin, updated quarterly  
- Rationale: Provides surge capacity during the 87.5-day mean lead time window and hedges against ANMAT registration delays

**2. Pre-Register Alternative Cisplatin Suppliers with ANMAT**  
- Initiate expedited ANMAT registration reviews **during stable supply periods**—not reactively during shortages  
- Identify 2–3 backup suppliers from alternative Indian or Chinese API manufacturers and obtain preliminary qualification  
- Reduces effective regulatory lag from 12–18 months to 2–4 months during actual disruptions

**3. Establish a Centralized, Real-Time Oncology Drug Inventory Dashboard**  
- Mandate reporting of stock levels from all major oncology dispensing points (public hospitals, obra social formularies, provincial systems) via a unified system  
- Modeled on INVIMA's "Desabastecimientos" module (Colombia) or MINSALUD's proposed SISCAATS platform  
- Enables identification of transferable stock and inter-channel loan logistics during shortages

**4. Formalize Inter-Hospital and Cross-Channel Loan Protocols**  
- Codify currently informal clinician-led drug loans between hospital networks and with provincial drug banks  
- Establish standing legal framework for emergency inter-service transfers during supply disruptions  
- Rationale: Currently semi-formal mechanisms resolve individual cases but are not scalable; formal protocols enable systematic logistics during a sector-wide shortage

**5. Coordinate Procurement Across Fragmented Channels**  
- Establish a **quarterly procurement coordination forum** with representatives from public hospitals, PAMI, obras sociales, and provincial health ministries  
- Aggregate demand forecasts and consolidate orders to maximize bargaining power and reduce fragmentation-driven visibility gaps  
- Reduces the effective budget constraint from structural austerity by enabling volume discounts

**6. Monitor for Shared-Supply-Chain Disruptions in Platinum-Based Agents**  
- Implement automated cross-drug alerts when cisplatin supply is disrupted—flag carboplatin availability simultaneously  
- Rationale: Cisplatin and carboplatin share the same India/China API supply chain; a disruption affecting one will affect the other, eliminating substitution flexibility

**7. Develop Contingency Clinical Protocols**  
- In consultation with oncology societies, establish and pre-approve **alternative regimen templates** (e.g., dose modifications, cycle delays, platinum-sparing substitutions where clinically acceptable) that can be activated during critical shortages (>14 consecutive stockout days predicted)  
- This supports clinical decision-making without requiring ad-hoc amparo filings

## Confidence & Limitations

**Strengths of This Assessment**

- The Monte Carlo simulation (500 runs, 365-day horizon) is grounded in queuing theory (Warren operations management framework) and incorporates both disruption and recovery phases following Badejo & Ierapetritou (2022)  
- Lead time, fill rate, and budget parameters are calibrated to Argentina's documented structural constraints (fiscal austerity floor of 75%, baseline procurement challenges) and the 2023–2024 cisplatin/carboplatin global shortage  
- The 7.9 stockout days projection is specific to Argentina and reflects its regulatory lag (ANMAT 12–18 months), fill rate (50% during disruption), and lead time (87.5 days)

**Key Uncertainties and Limitations**

1. **ANMAT Registration Acceleration is Assumed but Unverified**  
   - The model assumes ANMAT can expedite alternative supplier registration during crisis. In practice, this depends on political will and staffing capacity, which are not modeled. If registration remains at 12–18 months, effective lead time could extend to 100–200 days, worsening stockout projections substantially.

2. **Fill Rate Parameter (50%) is Scenario-Dependent**  
   - The 50% effective fill rate during disruption is based on industry observations from the 2023–2024 shortage but is not from Argentina-specific procurement contracts. Actual fill rates may vary by supplier and global demand allocation.

3. **No Clinical Demand Elasticity**  
   - The model assumes cisplatin demand remains constant (normal distribution, truncated at 0). In practice, prolonged shortages may trigger dose reductions, regimen substitutions, or treatment deferrals—reducing observed demand and partially mitigating shortage severity. This is not captured.
