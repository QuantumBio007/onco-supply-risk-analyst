## Drug Profile

Cisplatin is a platinum-based chemotherapy agent on the WHO Model List of Essential Medicines. It is generic and off-patent, with active pharmaceutical ingredient (API) manufacturing concentrated in India and China. The drug is essential for treatment of multiple cancer types (lung, testicular, ovarian, bladder, and others). Argentina sources cisplatin entirely through importation; there is no domestic API or finished-product manufacturing capacity.

## Supply Chain Vulnerability

**API Concentration Risk**
Cisplatin API supply is concentrated in two countries (India and China). Any export restriction, regulatory action, or geopolitical disruption simultaneously affects all generic manufacturers globally. Historical precedent exists: cisplatin shortages were documented in the United States in 2023 linked to manufacturing consolidation at Indian API suppliers.

**Generic Market Consolidation**
Price pressure in the generic market reduces manufacturer margins, leading some producers to exit the market. Single-source or dual-source API scenarios for specific formulations are common, amplifying vulnerability to any disruption of the two primary suppliers.

**Import-Dependent Procurement System**
Argentina has no alternative regional API source. All cisplatin supply flows through importation, creating a structural dependency on India/China API export availability. The Argentine procurement system comprises multiple independent channels (public hospitals, obras sociales, PAMI, private sector) with no integrated, real-time national inventory visibility. No single entity can determine, in operational time, which hospital or payer holds transferable available stock to resolve a therapeutic emergency.

**Currency and Budget Constraints**
Cisplatin is priced in USD globally. Argentina's procurement budgets are denominated in Argentine pesos. Currency devaluation episodes simultaneously reduce purchasing power across all channels (public hospitals, obras sociales, PAMI). The public channel and smaller obras sociales are most exposed. Suppliers facing delayed payments reduce credit extension to Argentine buyers, compressing distributor inventory levels downstream.

**Regulatory Friction**
ANMAT (Argentina's regulatory authority) requires active registration for all commercially distributed drugs. If the only registered manufacturer of a specific formulation exits the market, the standard supply pathway is interrupted until another manufacturer completes the registration process—a process that can take months to years. ANMAT maintains compassionate access mechanisms, but these do not function as operational replacements for population-level supply.

**Clinical and Access Implications**

The quantitative model suggests low overall supply risk under the API export restriction scenario. However, this aggregate projection masks a critical structural vulnerability specific to Argentina's fragmented, multi-channel health system:

1. **Unequal Access During Stockouts**: When a public hospital experiences a cisplatin stockout, patients without private insurance coverage cannot automatically access supply from obras sociales or prepaga channels. The formal supply pathway is treatment interruption or delay—the most clinically serious outcome of supply chain failure in oncology.

2. **Inventory Visibility Gap**: Modeled stockout days represent system-level average. The absence of a national inventory dashboard means that even during periods when aggregate stock exists, individual hospitals may face local depletion while other facilities hold transferable inventory. This visibility gap exacerbates real-world shortage duration for affected patients.

3. **Emergency Mechanism Dependence**: Access continuity relies on semi-formal mechanisms: direct purchases outside normal tender cycles, inter-service drug loans within hospital networks, coordination with provincial and national drug banks, and informal logistics coordinated by clinicians, hospital pharmacists, social workers, and patients' families. These mechanisms resolve many individual cases but do not function reliably at scale and are not operationalized for rapid response.

4. **Carboplatin Cross-Exposure**: Cisplatin and carboplatin (a related platinum agent) share the same API supply chain. Simultaneous disruption of both drugs is plausible under an export restriction scenario, eliminating an important clinical fallback option.

## Policy Recommendations

1. **Establish a national strategic reserve for cisplatin** (and carboplatin): A 60–90 day buffer stock at the national level would absorb the majority of the modeled disruption duration (~90 days). This reserve should be held in accessible locations (national drug bank, regional distribution hubs) and operationalized with clear protocols for rapid inter-hospital transfer.

2. **Implement national inventory visibility infrastructure**: Develop a near-real-time operational dashboard tracking cisplatin and carboplatin stock across public hospitals, obras sociales, provincial systems, and PAMI. This system must be distinct from regulatory traceability systems and designed for rapid decision-making during supply emergencies.

3. **Formalize emergency procurement pathways**: Codify and pre-authorize mechanisms for direct purchasing outside normal tender cycles, establish credit-line agreements with suppliers conditional on API export disruption, and clarify inter-payer inventory-sharing protocols.

4. **Coordinate procurement across channels**: Establish a standing working group (public hospitals, obras sociales, PAMI, provincial health authorities, ANMAT) to align demand forecasting, negotiate collective pricing, and reduce fragmentation that amplifies visibility gaps.

5. **Accelerate ANMAT registration for secondary manufacturers**: Proactively support ANMAT registration of additional cisplatin manufacturers to reduce single-source dependency and shorten re-registration timelines if a primary manufacturer exits.
