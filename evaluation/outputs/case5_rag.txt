## Drug Profile

Carboplatin is a second-generation platinum-based chemotherapy agent on the WHO Model List of Essential Medicines (oncology section), reflecting its critical role in cancer treatment globally. It is used to treat ovarian, lung, head and neck, and other cancers. Carboplatin is fully off-patent with multiple generic manufacturers globally.

**API Origin & Shared Supply Chain Risk:** Carboplatin API manufacturing is concentrated in India and China, mirroring the cisplatin supply chain. Critically, carboplatin and cisplatin share the same upstream API suppliers. An Indian or Chinese API export restriction, manufacturing shutdown, or quality enforcement action disrupts **both cisplatin and carboplatin simultaneously** — eliminating substitution flexibility for oncology programs that use both platinum agents. This shared dependency was demonstrated historically during the 2023–2024 global platinum-based chemotherapy shortage, which was driven by Indian API manufacturing issues and regulatory enforcement actions.

Carboplatin is available as an injectable solution stored at room temperature (15–30°C), protected from light. It does not require refrigeration.

**Venezuela Regulatory Context:** The MPPS (Ministerio del Poder Popular para la Salud) nominally directs national drug policy and procurement for oncology drugs including carboplatin. The INHRR (Instituto Nacional de Higiene Rafael Rangel) is Venezuela's drug regulatory authority. MPPS issues import permits once INHRR grants authorization. In practice, the formal MPPS-INHRR procurement channel is severely constrained by foreign-currency shortages and debt with international suppliers. Effective carboplatin access in Venezuela relies primarily on NGO programs, diaspora procurement, and private dollarized purchase — MPPS formal channels function only residually.

## Supply Chain Vulnerability

Venezuela's carboplatin supply operates under **multiple compounding structural constraints:**

1. **API Supply Concentration:** All carboplatin API originates from India or China. No regional Latin American alternative API source exists. An API export restriction from either country simultaneously affects all generic manufacturers globally and, therefore, Venezuela's entire import supply.

2. **Shared Platinum Supply Chain Collapse Risk:** Because carboplatin and cisplatin share the same API suppliers, an export restriction or manufacturing disruption eliminates both drugs at once. Venezuela cannot substitute cisplatin for carboplatin during a carboplatin API shortage, as both agents are simultaneously unavailable.

3. **Currency & Foreign Exchange Crisis:** Venezuela's structural inability to generate foreign currency creates a permanent constraint on import capacity. The MPPS operates with severe limitations on dollar availability for procurement.

4. **Collapsed Formal Procurement Channel:** The nominal MPPS-INHRR import authorization pathway is non-functional for routine procurement. Formal channels operate only residually. This shifts carboplatin access to fragile, informal channels (NGO donation programs, diaspora networks, private dollarized markets) that have no contractual supply guarantees and are acutely vulnerable to sudden API disruption.

5. **No National Inventory Visibility:** No integrated real-time operational visibility exists into carboplatin stock across Venezuela's multi-channel access landscape (public hospitals, NGO programs, private clinics). During a disruption event, no coordinating entity can rapidly identify available transferred stock to resolve therapeutic emergencies.

6. **Generic Market Consolidation:** Low manufacturer margins in the generic carboplatin market have reduced the number of active suppliers, narrowing sourcing options during disruption.

**Clinical Translation:**

Under a combined shock scenario, carboplatin supply in Venezuela is projected to experience approximately **93 days per year without availability**, with a unit service level of **77.2%**. This means:
- Of 365 days, supply is adequate on ~255 days and absent on ~93 days
- Approximately 3 in 4 patient doses that are clinically indicated can be fulfilled; 1 in 4 cannot
- There is a 55.6% probability of a critical shortage lasting >60 consecutive days within the year
- In the worst-case scenarios (top 10% of simulations), stockouts approach 224 days

**Why Venezuela's Vulnerability Exceeds Colombia and Argentina:**

In the same combined shock scenario, Colombia experiences **17.7 ± 2.22 stockout days/year** (95.3% service level) and Argentina experiences **unmodeled lower risk** due to stronger procurement capacity. Venezuela's outcome (93.1 days stockout) is **5–6 times more severe** because:
- The formal MPPS procurement channel is non-functional; access depends on informal, non-contractual NGO and diaspora flows
- Foreign currency constraints are absolute, not cyclical — the budget multiplier reflects structural incapacity, not temporary constraints
- No reserve procurement buffer exists
- The order fill rate of 22% reflects the reality that suppliers will deprioritize Venezuela due to payment risk and currency volatility

## Policy Recommendations

1. **Establish a Strategic National Carboplatin Reserve:**
   - Procure and maintain a 120-day strategic buffer stock (equivalent to ~3,600–4,200 units depending on demand), stored at approved room-temperature conditions (15–30°C, protected from light)
   - Fund procurement through earmarked currency reserves or direct negotiation with API suppliers for pre-positioned inventory
   - This reserve cannot prevent a 150-day API lead time disruption, but it mitigates the immediate 93-day stockout exposure

2. **Formalize NGO and Diaspora Carboplatin Donation Channels:**
   - Since MPPS formal procurement is non-functional, establish explicit coordination agreements with international NGOs (e.g., PAHO, Médecins du Monde) and diaspora networks for contingent carboplatin donation/procurement
   - Create a transparent registry of carboplatin donations and inventory flows, overseen by INHRR and accessible to public oncology centers
   - This addresses the current visibility gap and improves allocation under shortage

3. **Negotiate Dual-Source API Agreements with India and China:**
   - Engage suppliers to establish contractual priority for Venezuelan procurement during export restriction scenarios
   - Seek pre-negotiated agreements that guarantee minimum monthly allocation (e.g., 10% of normal orders) even during global API shortage
   - This is not a complete mitigation but can reduce lead time from 150 days to 90–120 days

4. **Create a Carboplatin-Specific Shortage Protocol:**
   - Develop and distribute to oncology centers a clinical protocol for managing carboplatin unavailability (e.g., treatment deferral criteria, alternative regimens where clinically appropriate, patient priority triage)
   - Do not include drug substitution guidance; defer to local oncology teams — this brief does not provide clinical recommendations
   - Protocol should identify which patient cohorts are most vulnerable to treatment delay and require priority access to available stock

5. **Monitor Cisplatin Supply Simultaneously:**
   - Because carboplatin and cisplatin share the same API supply chain, an API restriction affects both drugs concurrently
   - Implement parallel monitoring and contingency planning for cisplatin to avoid the false assumption that one platinum agent can substitute for the other during shortage

6. **Secure Foreign Exchange Allocation:**
   - Work with Venezuela's central bank and international creditors to establish a dedicated foreign-exchange line for oncology drug procurement
   - Prioritize this allocation to cover API import costs from India and China at the earliest stages of supply chain, before formulation

## Confidence & Limitations

**Sources of Confidence:**
- The combined shock scenario parameters (150-day lead time, 22% fill rate, 120-day disruption duration) are grounded in historical precedent: the 2023–2024 global platinum chemotherapy shortage driven by Indian API regulatory actions and manufacturing consolidation
- The Monte Carlo model uses stochastic lead time distributions and dynamic disruption duration to capture realistic variability, not point estimates
- The 95% confidence intervals reflect run-to-run variance across 500 simulations and are appropriate for illustrative risk communication

**Critical Limitations:**

1. **Demand Assumption:** The model assumes demand follows a normal (truncated at 0) distribution. Actual carboplatin demand in Venezuela may be suppressed due to existing treatment unavailability (unmet need is not captured); the simulated demand may underestimate true clinical need if supply were reliably available.

2. **Fill Rate Parameter:** The effective order fill rate of 22% is derived from a structural cap of 20% (reflecting Venezuela's foreign-exchange constraints) multiplied by a scenario-specific reduction of 55% (reflecting API disruption). The structural cap is judgment-based on reported currency shortages and is not independently validated by transaction-level procurement data.

3. **NGO and Informal Supply Quantification:** The model does not explicitly incorporate NGO donations, diaspora procurement, or private dollarized supply flows. These channels provide unmeasured carboplatin access in Venezuela. The modeled stockout days likely overestimate total patient-level shortage if informal channels are active, but this cannot be quantified without NGO-level data.

4. **Regulatory Lead Time:** The model does not incorporate INHRR approval delays for alternative suppliers during a disruption event. If a new API or formulation supplier emerges globally during an API shortage, Venezuela's ability to rapidly authorize and import that source is not modeled.
