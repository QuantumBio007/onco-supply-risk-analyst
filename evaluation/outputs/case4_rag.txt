## Drug Profile

Doxorubicin is an anthracycline chemotherapy agent used to treat breast cancer, leukemia, lymphoma, and sarcomas. It is included on the WHO Model List of Essential Medicines (oncology section) and is off-patent and fully generic. The primary global suppliers of doxorubicin API are India (dominant) and China (secondary). Colombia has no domestic API manufacturing capacity and is entirely import-dependent on these two countries for doxorubicin supply.

In Colombia, doxorubicin is PBS-covered (Plan de Beneficios en Salud, Resolution 2718/2024) and is procured through the EPS (health insurers) and pharmaceutical distributors (Audifarma, Cruz Verde, Cafam, Colsubsidio, Coopidrogas) to supply hospital IPS networks. The drug is supplied as an injectable solution and powder for reconstitution, requiring refrigeration at 2–8°C and protection from light — a cold chain dependency that amplifies the impact of logistics disruptions.

## Supply Chain Vulnerability

**API Concentration Risk**  
Doxorubicin API manufacturing is concentrated in India and China, which together account for an estimated 60–70% of global generic pharmaceutical API supply. Any regulatory action, export restriction, pandemic-related shutdown, or geopolitical event affecting these two countries simultaneously disrupts all global generic manufacturers, including those supplying Colombia. Latin America has no regional API manufacturing backup.

**Regulatory Constraint**  
All doxorubicin suppliers in Colombia must maintain an active INVIMA (Instituto Nacional de Vigilancia de Medicamentos y Alimentos) sanitary registration before commercial distribution. As of 2024, INVIMA maintains a backlog of over 14,000 pending registrations across all product categories. During a supply disruption, alternative suppliers without INVIMA registration cannot rapidly substitute, effectively extending lead times by 12–18 months.

**Currency Devaluation Impact**  
Doxorubicin is priced in USD in international trade. Colombian hospital procurement budgets are denominated in Colombian pesos. Currency devaluation directly reduces the purchasing power of EPS and public hospitals buying imported doxorubicin, leading to:
- Reduced order volumes
- Deferred purchases
- Increased stockout risk, particularly in public hospital networks with fixed peso budgets

**Cold Chain Vulnerability**  
The 2–8°C refrigeration requirement creates an additional layer of risk: port delays, customs holds, and power outages threaten not only supply availability but also product integrity. This is especially acute in Colombia given infrastructure constraints in logistics nodes.

**Procurement System Fragmentation**  
Colombia's two-regime insurance system (contributory and subsidized) involves 28 EPS entities and multiple pharmaceutical distributors and hospital networks. This fragmentation creates visibility gaps — no single entity tracks national doxorubicin stock levels — making coordinated response to supply shocks difficult.

**Mechanism**  
The currency shock reduces effective order quantities by approximately 52% (from 80% to 48% effective budget), as EPS and hospital networks command fewer USD to purchase imported doxorubicin. However, the modeled impact is modest because:
1. Doxorubicin demand is clinically inelastic — oncology patients cannot defer treatment
2. The EPS capitation system (UPC disbursement by ADRES) is designed to protect PBS-covered drugs like doxorubicin from budget shocks
3. The 75% fill rate reflects chronic structural procurement failures (~17% of orders fail or arrive short) independent of currency devaluation

## Policy Recommendations

1. **Establish a strategic buffer stock of 30–45 days** at the national level (coordinated across ADRES and major EPS) to absorb currency-driven procurement delays and regulatory registration bottlenecks. Current modeled inventory (123 units) represents approximately 15–20 days of consumption.

2. **Pre-register alternative doxorubicin suppliers with INVIMA proactively**, rather than during a shortage. Given the 12–18 month registration timeline and the 14,000+ application backlog, advance approval of backup suppliers from India and China reduces regulatory lag during API supply disruptions.

3. **Index doxorubicin procurement contracts to USD-denominated safety stock thresholds** within the EPS/ADRES capitation system. During currency devaluation, automatically trigger proportional increases in UPC disbursement for doxorubicin to maintain order volumes at baseline levels.

4. **Implement real-time supply visibility across the EPS network** through a centralized reporting system (e.g., linked to MIPRES and INVIMA databases) to identify regional stockouts before they cascade into treatment delays. Current fragmentation prevents coordinated action.

5. **Negotiate API supply agreements with Indian manufacturers that include a minimum 60-day advance notice clause** for any export restrictions or production interruptions, allowing Colombian importers time to activate alternative suppliers or increase domestic buffer stock.

6. **Strengthen cold chain infrastructure** at critical nodes (ports, distribution centers, hospital pharmacies) to prevent product loss during extended supply-chain delays, particularly in public hospital networks in resource-constrained regions.

## Confidence & Limitations

**What is confident:**
- Doxorubicin's WHO EML status, off-patent generic status, and PBS coverage in Colombia are documented.
- India and China dominate global doxorubicin API manufacturing; this is an established structural fact.
- Colombia's INVIMA registration requirement and backlog (14,000+ applications) are confirmed as of 2024.
- The currency devaluation scenario is modeled on a peso devaluation of 60%, with corresponding 52% reduction in USD-denominated purchasing power.

**What is uncertain:**
- The stockout projections (9.0 days/year ±0.76) are outputs from a simplified inventory simulation model, not empirical audit data. Actual stockouts depend on unmeasured variables: EPS contract terms with distributors, informal supply channels, regional redistribution between hospitals, and clinical triage decisions.
- The 75% fill rate assumption reflects chronic structural procurement failures (~17% of orders fail or arrive short), but this figure is derived from indirect evidence, not a comprehensive audit of Colombian EPS pharmaceutical procurement.
- The model assumes disruption duration as a geometric random variable with mean=180 days, reflecting finite recovery dynamics. Actual currency devaluation may persist longer or be offset by monetary policy intervention, making the modeled duration speculative.
- No primary data on current doxorubicin inventory levels in Colombian EPS or public hospitals is available; the "123 units" average is a model output, not an observed stock count.
- The interaction between currency devaluation and concurrent API supply restrictions (e.g., Indian export ban) is not modeled; joint scenarios could produce non-linear effects.

**Limitations:**
- This brief does not provide clinical guidance on drug substitution or alternative oncology regimens.
- The analysis assumes the PBS coverage and EPS capitation system remain stable; changes to Colombian health policy could alter risk profiles materially.
- Regional variation within Colombia (public vs. private sector, urban vs. rural hospitals, departmental health authority capacity) is not captured.
