## Drug Profile
Doxorubicin is an anthracycline antibiotic chemotherapy agent used to treat breast cancer, leukemia, lymphoma, and sarcomas. It is on the WHO Model List of Essential Medicines (oncology section). Conventional doxorubicin is fully off-patent and generic. API manufacturing is dominated by India, with secondary supply from China. Colombia has no domestic API manufacturing capacity and is fully import-dependent on Indian and Chinese suppliers. Doxorubicin is available as injectable solution and powder for reconstitution, requiring cold-chain storage at 2–8°C and protection from light. In Colombia, doxorubicin requires active INVIMA (Instituto Nacional de Vigilancia de Medicamentos y Alimentos) sanitary registration before legal commercial distribution; as of 2024, INVIMA maintained a backlog of over 14,000 pending registrations across all product categories, constraining supplier substitution during disruptions.

## Supply Chain Vulnerability
Colombia's doxorubicin supply chain faces three overlapping vulnerabilities:

1. **API Concentration Risk**: India is the single dominant source; China is secondary. Global shortages among Indian and Chinese API producers (documented in 2011–2012 and 2022–2023) cascade directly into Colombia because the same suppliers serve both developed and Latin American markets.

2. **Regulatory Constraint on Substitution**: INVIMA registration is mandatory for legal supply. Without it, alternative suppliers cannot rapidly substitute during disruptions, creating a regulatory bottleneck independent of physical supply availability.

3. **Currency-Linked Budget Compression**: Doxorubicin is priced in USD in international trade. Colombian hospital procurement budgets are denominated in Colombian pesos. Currency devaluation directly reduces the purchasing power of EPS (Entidades Promotoras de Salud) and IPS entities, leading to reduced order volumes, deferred purchases, and increased stockout risk—particularly acute in public hospitals with fixed peso budgets. The EPS-distributor financial debt cascade (COP 4.2 trillion pharmaceutical debt outstanding as of 2024, with EPS-to-distributor payment cycles extending 180–365+ days) amplifies this effect by restricting actual drug shipments regardless of nominal funding.

**Interpretation:**
Under this scenario, doxorubicin supply in Colombia is projected to experience **within acceptable operational range** (8.9 stockout days/year, 97.7% service level), though monitoring is warranted. The 48% budget multiplier indicates that currency devaluation reduces effective procurement purchasing power by approximately half, compressing order quantities. The 75% fill rate reflects both scenario-specific budget constraints and chronic structural failures in Colombia's EPS-IPS-distributor system. The 80% probability of *any* stockout during the year signals that disruptions occur but remain below critical thresholds under this specific scenario.

## Policy Recommendations

1. **Establish a national strategic doxorubicin reserve**: Procure and maintain 60–90 days of buffer stock at the centralized pharmacy level (SISCAATS-integrated) to offset currency-linked procurement cycles and distributor payment delays.

2. **Accelerate INVIMA registration pathways**: Reduce the 14,000+ registration backlog by fast-tracking oncology drugs on the PBS formulary. Pre-qualification of alternative doxorubicin suppliers (both API and finished-form) will improve substitutability during disruptions.

3. **Ring-fence oncology pharmaceutical budgets against currency fluctuations**: Use a currency hedge or USD-indexed allocation mechanism for doxorubicin and other essential imports, so that devaluation does not automatically compress order volumes.

4. **Strengthen EPS-distributor settlement discipline**: Enforce the nominal 30–60 day payment cycle through ADRES oversight and SuperSalud sanctions. The observed 180–365+ day cycles directly reduce effective supply availability regardless of nominal funding.

5. **Implement SISCAATS early for doxorubicin**: Integrate doxorubicin into the new Sistema de Información para la Centralización de Alertas de Abastecimiento de Tecnologías en Salud ahead of the full 6-month implementation window to enable real-time visibility and early intervention.

## Confidence & Limitations

**Confidence:**
- The stockout-day projections (8.9 ± 0.7 days) are based on continuous-review (Q, r) inventory modeling with Monte Carlo simulation (500 runs) under explicitly stated parameter assumptions (lead times, fill rates, budget multiplier, disruption duration).
- The retrieved context confirms Colombia's full import dependence on Indian and Chinese doxorubicin API, INVIMA's regulatory role, and the currency mechanism linking peso devaluation to budget compression.
- The 75% effective fill rate reflects documented structural procurement failures in Colombia (~17% order failure rate due to fiscal constraints), cited in the same context.

**Limitations:**
- **Model simplification**: The inventory model does not capture nonlinear effects of the EPS financial debt cascade (COP 4.2 trillion outstanding) on actual distributor willingness to ship, beyond the 75% fill-rate proxy. Real-world shortages may exceed modeled estimates if EPS insolvencies accelerate.
- **Regulatory uncertainty**: The impact of INVIMA's 14,000+ registration backlog on substitutability is qualitatively described but not quantified. The time-to-registration for alternative suppliers is not specified in the retrieved context.
- **Scenario boundary**: This brief models only currency devaluation; concurrent macroeconomic shocks (inflation, oil price spikes, debt crisis) are addressed separately in other scenario analyses. Combined scenarios may produce different outcomes.
- **Cold-chain risk**: The brief does not quantify the impact of logistics disruptions (port delays, customs holds, power outages) on cold-chain integrity, though the context identifies this as a direct threat to product viability.
- **Demand variation**: The model assumes normal (truncated) demand distribution; actual cancer treatment demand in Colombia may exhibit seasonal or policy-driven clustering not captured here.
