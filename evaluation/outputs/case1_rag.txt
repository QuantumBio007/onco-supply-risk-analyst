## Drug Profile

Cisplatin is a platinum-based alkylating chemotherapy agent used to treat testicular, ovarian, bladder, lung, and head and neck cancers. It has been in clinical use since 1978 and is **on the WHO Model List of Essential Medicines (oncology section) since 1979**, signaling that countries should treat it as a procurement priority. 

Cisplatin is fully off-patent with multiple generic manufacturers worldwide. The overwhelming majority of cisplatin API is manufactured in **India and China**, which together account for over 80% of global supply. No significant API manufacturing exists in Latin America; Argentina is fully import-dependent.

Cisplatin is available as an injectable solution or powder for reconstitution, stored at room temperature (15–25°C) and protected from light. Critically, it should **NOT be refrigerated**, as refrigeration causes precipitation and renders the product unusable—a storage requirement distinct from many other oncology injectables.

## Supply Chain Vulnerability

**Concentrated API sourcing:** The overwhelming dependence on Indian and Chinese API suppliers creates systemic vulnerability. Any export restriction, regulatory action, manufacturing shutdown, or geopolitical disruption simultaneously affects all generic manufacturers globally, with no alternative regional API source in Latin America.

**Generic market dynamics:** Price competition among generic manufacturers reduces margins and has driven consolidation in the API supplier base, historically increasing shortage risk. Recent US FDA-forced plant shutdowns at Indian API suppliers triggered cisplatin/carboplatin global shortages in 2023–2024.

**Regulatory constraint:** ANMAT (Administración Nacional de Medicamentos, Alimentos y Tecnología Médica) must approve any new supplier or alternative manufacturer before legal distribution in Argentina. Standard ANMAT registration timelines are 12–18 months for generic drugs. During an API supply disruption, this regulatory lag extends the practical procurement lead time by 6–18 months and becomes a critical constraint.

**Structural access gap:** Argentina has no single entity with real-time, integrated visibility into national oncology drug stock across all channels (public hospitals, obras sociales, PAMI, prepagas, provincial systems). The gap between formal drug coverage and effective patient access is a structural feature of the system. Clinical authorization, reimbursement file processing, supplier contracting, and dispensing logistics each introduce latency and failure points independent of aggregate drug availability. This fragmentation means that centralized procurement data does not reliably predict on-ground access.

**Shared platinum API supply chain:** Cisplatin and carboplatin share the same API supply chain (India and China primary sources). An API export restriction, manufacturing shutdown, or quality enforcement action disrupts both platinum agents simultaneously, eliminating substitution flexibility for oncology programs using both drugs.

However, a 74% probability of experiencing any stockout during the year indicates occasional supply-demand friction even under normal conditions—consistent with documented access delays in Argentina's multi-channel system.

**Interpretation:** Under baseline conditions, cisplatin supply in Argentina is projected to experience roughly one week of stockout days per year. This is within acceptable operational range but reflects chronic structural constraints: import-dependent sourcing, regulatory lead times, procurement fragmentation across public/private channels, and the 75% effective budget cap that constrains reorder quantities. The 7-day baseline is not equivalent to zero risk—it signals that even without API disruption, the system operates at its performance ceiling.

## Policy Recommendations

1. **Establish a national strategic cisplatin reserve** of 60–90 days of demand (approximately 180–270 units based on modeled average inventory). This buffer should be held centrally by a single entity (e.g., Ministry of Health or BNDE) and accessible to public hospitals and contracted providers during stockout events. This addresses the regulatory lag inherent in ANMAT approval of alternative suppliers during disruptions.

2. **Create an integrated national oncology drug visibility dashboard** with real-time stock reporting from all major procuring channels (public hospitals, obras sociales networks, provincial drug banks, PAMI). This should include a rapid-alert mechanism when aggregate stock approaches the reorder point. Current traceability systems allow reconstruction of movements; a real-time operational dashboard is feasible and would eliminate the structural access gap documented in the Argentina procurement analysis.

3. **Coordinate inter-channel procurement** through a standing committee (Ministry of Health, ANMAT, major obras sociales, hospital networks) to standardize cisplatin supply contracts and reduce fragmentation. Align reorder timing across channels to increase collective order volume and negotiating leverage with suppliers, offsetting the 75% effective budget multiplier constraint.

4. **Negotiate advance supply commitments** with at least two independent cisplatin API suppliers (prioritizing diversification away from India/China duopoly, if possible—e.g., exploring suppliers in other regions with ANMAT-equivalent regulatory frameworks). Contractually secure a minimum 30-day rolling inventory commitment during normal conditions and an accelerated reorder protocol with <45-day lead time for emergency orders.

5. **Establish formal inter-service drug-loan protocols** across hospital networks and provincial drug banks to standardize and scale the informal logistics currently used during stockouts. Codify decision rules for protocol shifts (e.g., treatment cycle delays, dose adjustments) and ensure clinical documentation and reimbursement workflows support these mechanisms rather than penalizing hospitals for shortages.

6. **Monitor carboplatin supply jointly** with cisplatin, given the shared platinum API supply chain. Any shortage of one should trigger contingency planning for both to prevent therapeutic bottlenecks.

## Confidence & Limitations

**Model assumptions:**
- The Monte Carlo simulation uses a continuous-review (Q, r) inventory model with stochastic lead times and is calibrated to Argentina-specific parameters (lead time distribution, fill rates, budget constraints). However, it represents a single hospital or integrated procurement system, not the fragmented multi-channel Argentine reality. Aggregating across channels may smooth simulated variability but does not capture the access gaps documented in real clinical practice.
- Demand distribution is modeled as normal (truncated at zero), which is standard for mature oncology drugs but does not account for episodic surges due to cancer case clustering or treatment protocol changes.
- The 86% effective fill rate and 75% budget multiplier are structural parameters derived from Argentina's documented procurement constraints. They are not time-varying and do not capture year-on-year inflation, exchange rate volatility (relevant for imports), or policy changes to obra social reimbursement rates.

**Data gaps:**
- The context does not provide Argentina-specific demand data (units/year), prices, or actual stockout event logs. Simulated baseline stockout days (7.0/year) are illustrative, not actuarial estimates.
- No granular data on ANMAT approval timelines for specific cisplatin suppliers or historical cases of registration delays during global shortages.
- The 75% effective budget cap and 86% fill rate are derived from Argentina's historical procurement context but are not validated against recent (2024–2025) real-time order data.

**Structural limitations:**
- The brief does not and cannot quantify the impact of treatment delays, dose modifications, or amparo de salud filings on clinical outcomes. These are significant but outside the scope of supply chain analysis.
- The fragmentation of Argentina's procurement system means that a national-level model, however refined, systematically underestimates access risk at the patient level. Real-world shortage severity depends on which hospital or payer runs out of stock and whether alternative channels (inter-service loans, informal sourcing) are available locally.
- Regulatory lead time (ANMAT 12–18 months) is a known constraint but is not explicitly modeled in the baseline simulation. The simulation assumes lead time of 35 days on average under normal conditions; in a real disruption requiring new supplier registration, the effective lead time would extend by 6–18 months, fundamentally changing the risk profile.

**Confidence level:** The baseline service level projection (98.3%) is high-confidence within the model's scope. The structural vulnerabilities (API concentration, regulatory lag, budget constraints, channel fragmentation) are documented in the retrieved context and high-confidence. Confidence in translating this brief into specific procurement actions is **moderate**—it depends on data availability and institutional coordination that are not fully characterized in the source material.
