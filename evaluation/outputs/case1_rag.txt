## Drug Profile

Cisplatin is a platinum-based alkylating chemotherapy agent used to treat testicular, ovarian, bladder, lung, and head and neck cancers. It has been in clinical use since 1978 and is **on the WHO Model List of Essential Medicines since 1979**, confirming its status as a globally essential medicine and procurement priority.

Cisplatin is fully off-patent with multiple generic manufacturers worldwide. **Primary API manufacturing is concentrated in India and China, which together account for over 80% of global cisplatin API supply.** No significant API manufacturing exists in Latin America; Argentina is fully import-dependent.

The drug is available as an injectable solution or powder for reconstitution and must be stored at room temperature (15–25°C), protected from light. Critically, cisplatin must **not** be refrigerated, as refrigeration causes precipitation and renders the product unusable—a storage requirement distinct from many other oncology injectables.

---

## Supply Chain Vulnerability

### Import Dependency & Upstream Concentration
Argentina relies entirely on imported cisplatin API from India and China. No regional manufacturing alternative exists. Any export restriction, regulatory action, or geopolitical disruption originating in these two countries simultaneously affects all generic suppliers globally, with no substitution flexibility at the API level.

### Shared Platinum-Based Supply Chain
**Cisplatin and carboplatin share the same API supply chain.** Both drugs' active pharmaceutical ingredients are manufactured primarily in India and secondarily in China. This means an Indian or Chinese API export restriction, manufacturing shutdown, or quality enforcement action disrupts **both cisplatin and carboplatin simultaneously**, eliminating substitution flexibility for oncology programs that use both platinum agents.

### Generic Market Dynamics
Generic market price competition reduces manufacturer margins, leading some producers to exit the market. Single-source or dual-source API scenarios for specific formulations are common. The United States experienced a documented cisplatin shortage linked to manufacturing consolidation at Indian API suppliers (2023).

### Fragmented Procurement System
Argentina's oncology drug procurement operates through **eight independent channels** without central coordination or real-time shared inventory visibility:
- National and provincial public systems (25 parallel systems, one per province plus CABA)
- Public hospital independent pharmacy networks
- Obras sociales (social insurance funds)
- PAMI (state pensioner insurance)
- Prepagas (private health plans)
- Private retail pharmacies
- PAHO Strategic Fund
- Judicial access (amparo de salud)

Even when aggregate national supply is adequate, individual channels can stockout while others hold surplus. **No single entity can determine in operational time which hospital or payer holds transferable available stock** to resolve a therapeutic emergency.

### Regulatory Lead Time Constraint
In Argentina, ANMAT (Administración Nacional de Medicamentos, Alimentos y Tecnología Médica) must approve any new supplier or alternative manufacturer before legal distribution of cisplatin. Standard ANMAT registration timelines are approximately **12–18 months for generic drugs.** During an API export restriction scenario, this regulatory clearance requirement becomes a critical constraint: even if alternative suppliers exist globally, they cannot supply Argentina until ANMAT registration is complete, effectively extending practical lead time by 6–18 months during supply disruptions.

### Fiscal Constraints
Argentina's procurement budgets are set in Argentine pesos while drugs are priced in USD, creating chronic currency exposure. Fiscal capacity varies dramatically across provinces: CABA and Buenos Aires Province have dedicated high-cost drug bureaus, while smaller provinces (Formosa, Chaco, Santiago del Estero, La Rioja) report chronic chemotherapy stockouts. Approximately **36% of the population relies solely on the public sector.**

## Scenario Impact Analysis

### Baseline Conditions (Normal Operations)

Under baseline conditions (current normal supplier performance, no geopolitical disruption, standard demand patterns, and chronic fiscal constraints):

- **Stockout days per year: 6.0 days** (95% CI: ±0.57 days)
- **Unit service level: 98.5%**
- **Probability of any stockout during the year: 69%**
- **Probability of critical shortage (>60 stockout days): 0.0%**
- **Average inventory on hand: 211 units**
- **Overall supply risk: LOW**

### Interpretation

Cisplatin supply in Argentina under baseline conditions is projected to be **within acceptable operational range**, though monitoring is warranted. The 6.0 stockout days per year reflect routine supply-demand mismatches driven by:
- 86% supplier fill rate (normal performance), reflecting generic market dynamics and manufacturing lead times
- 75% effective procurement budget (chronic fiscal constraints in Argentina independent of any scenario shock)
- Fragmentation across eight procurement channels reducing inventory visibility and redistribution capacity

The high probability of at least one stockout event during the year (69%) reflects the structural fragmentation of the system—not global supply failure, but local channel depletion. The absence of critical shortage risk (0% probability of >60 stockout days) indicates that under baseline conditions, no single channel experiences sustained stockout.

## Policy Recommendations

1. **Establish a national oncology drug inventory dashboard** with real-time visibility across all eight procurement channels (public hospitals, obras sociales, PAMI, prepagas, private retail, PAHO, provincial systems, and judicial access routes). This does not require centralized procurement—only operational-time data-sharing to enable emergency stock redistribution.

2. **Create a provincial-level cisplatin buffer stock mechanism** coordinated through COFESA (Consejo Federal de Salud) to support smaller provinces (Formosa, Chaco, Santiago del Estero, La Rioja) that report chronic chemotherapy stockouts. Target: 30-day minimum buffer at provincial hubs.

3. **Accelerate ANMAT pre-registration of alternative cisplatin suppliers** during baseline periods (not during shortages). Maintain a standing list of pre-approved, quality-assured backup suppliers with completed regulatory dossiers, reducing emergency lead time from 12–18 months to 30–60 days should the primary supplier fail.

4. **De-couple procurement budgets from currency fluctuations** by establishing a dedicated USD-denominated allocation for essential oncology medicines (including cisplatin, carboplatin, and doxorubicin) within the public health budget, insulating these critical drugs from peso devaluation cycles.

5. **Strengthen inter-channel coordination for critical shortages** by formalizing a high-level protocol (similar to crisis-management agreements in other sectors) that allows emergency stock transfers from obras sociales or PAMI reserves to public hospital channels during documented local depletion, with reimbursement mechanisms post-shortage.

6. **Monitor shared platinum-based supply chain risk** by tracking both cisplatin and carboplatin API availability simultaneously. Any signal of restriction or disruption in one drug should trigger contingency procurement for both, given the shared India-China supply chain and high correlation (r≈0.87) in simultaneous disruption risk.

## Confidence & Limitations

### Data & Methods
- Baseline stockout projections (6.0 days/year) are derived from a single-echelon inventory model applied to aggregate Argentine cisplatin demand. The model treats the entire supply chain as one inventory node at the hospital/EPS level and is appropriate for generating shortage risk signals at the point of care but **cannot distinguish between distributor-level shortages and end-user depletion** across the eight fragmented channels.

- The 86% fill rate assumption reflects normal supplier performance under current market conditions (as of 2024–2025) but is not a long-term historical average. Actual fill rates may vary with global cisplatin API market dynamics and consolidation trends.

- The 75% effective budget multiplier reflects chronic fiscal constraints in Argentina (oil revenue collapse, structural austerity) as documented in the R3 inventory loop literature. This baseline assumption does not account for unexpected currency crises or further budget reductions.

### Scenario Boundaries
- This brief addresses baseline conditions only. Separate scenario analyses are required for API export restriction, currency devaluation, and combined shocks.
- Baseline projections assume normal ANMAT/regulatory processing times and do not model disruptions caused by regulatory compliance failures or quality holds at suppliers.

### Procurement System Complexity
- The fragmentation of Argentine oncology procurement across eight independent channels cannot be fully captured in a single-echelon model. Real shortages may occur in specific channels (e.g., a public hospital in a smaller province) while other channels hold surplus stock. The brief quantifies **average national** risk, not channel-specific risk.

- The gap between formal coverage (nominal inclusion in obras sociales or PAMI benefit packages) and effective access (actual physical delivery to patient) is a structural feature of the Argentine system. Shortages reported clinically may reflect access delays rather than true supply depletion.

### WHO EML Status & Policy Implications
- Cisplatin's inclusion on the WHO Model List of Essential Medicines since 1979 signals that Argentina should treat it as a procurement priority. However, EML status does not confer supply guarantees; it establishes an international norm that shortages represent a policy failure, not merely a supply chain event. The brief does not assess whether Argentina's current procurement fragmentation constitutes policy failure under this standard.

### Regional Context
- The FIFARMA WAIT 2024 indicator documents that only 35% of internationally approved medicines have full or partial public availability through Argentina's formal health system, and mean access lag to approved medicines is 4.75 years across Latin America. This regional access gap is external to cisplatin-specific supply chain analysis but contextualizes the chronic fiscal and regulatory constraints embedded in the 75% budget multiplier.
