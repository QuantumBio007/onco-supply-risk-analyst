## Drug Profile

Cisplatin is a platinum-based chemotherapy agent on the WHO Model List of Essential Medicines. It is generic and off-patent, with active pharmaceutical ingredient (API) manufacturing concentrated in two countries: India and China. Generic manufacturers globally source from this dual-source supply base, creating synchronized vulnerability across all regional markets. Argentina imports cisplatin formulations; no domestic API or finished-product manufacturing exists.

## Supply Chain Vulnerability

**API Supply Concentration**  
Cisplatin API supply is concentrated in India and China. Any export restriction, regulatory action, or geopolitical disruption simultaneously affects all generic manufacturers globally. Historical precedent exists: cisplatin shortages were reported in the United States in 2023, linked to manufacturing consolidation at Indian API suppliers.

**Argentine Procurement Landscape**  
Argentina's oncology procurement is fragmented across multiple channels: public hospitals, obras sociales (employer-based insurance), PAMI (retiree insurance), and private prepaid systems. This multi-channel structure creates structural visibility gaps. No single entity in Argentina maintains complete, real-time operational visibility into national oncology drug stock across all channels. Regulatory traceability systems exist through ANMAT (Administración Nacional de Medicamentos, Alimentos y Tecnología Médica), but traceability is not equivalent to actionable inventory visibility. A critical distinction: no public body can operationally determine, in real time, which hospital or payer holds transferable available stock to resolve a therapeutic emergency.

**Currency and Budget Constraints**  
Cisplatin is priced in USD on global markets. Argentine procurement budgets across public hospitals, obras sociales, and PAMI are denominated in Argentine pesos. Each devaluation episode simultaneously reduces purchasing power across the entire system. The effective budget constraint modeled in the baseline scenario is 75%, reflecting chronic fiscal constraints in Argentina independent of any scenario shock. Public hospitals and smaller obras sociales are most exposed; larger entities like PAMI retain some negotiating leverage but remain peso-constrained. Suppliers facing delayed payments reduce credit extension to Argentine importers, compressing distributor inventory levels downstream.

**Access Fragmentation**  
When a public hospital stock-out occurs, patients are not automatically redirected to other channels. The institutional sequence involves attempting to source within the same hospital or provincial network, consulting provincial/national drug banks (bancos de drogas), temporarily modifying treatment protocol if a clinically acceptable alternative exists, delaying treatment cycles, or transferring to another public hospital. In severe cases, administrative escalation or judicial action (judicialización) may be pursued. Semi-formal mechanisms—inter-service drug loans, inter-hospital coordination, and direct purchases outside tender cycles—partially resolve individual cases but do not substitute for population-level supply continuity.

**Interpretation**  
The baseline scenario projects service levels above 98%, which falls within operationally acceptable range. However, the 74% probability of experiencing at least one stockout event during the year indicates that individual stock-outs are anticipated within normal operations — a background risk rather than an exceptional failure mode. The modeling framework explicitly accounts for Argentine structural constraints (75% budget utilization, fragmented procurement channels, currency exposure) but assumes stable global supply and normal lead times.

The 7 stockout days/year represents the residual risk even under optimal inventory policy (safety stock of 117 units, reorder point of 397 units). This baseline establishes the floor risk profile against which disruption scenarios (API export restriction, regulatory action) are compared.

## Policy Recommendations

1. **Establish National Visibility Infrastructure**  
   Develop an operational (not merely regulatory) inventory dashboard integrating real-time stock data across public hospitals, PAMI, obras sociales, and major private systems. Distinguish between traceability (post-hoc unit tracking) and actionable inventory visibility (real-time localization of transferable stock for therapeutic emergencies).

2. **Implement Strategic Reserve for Cisplatin**  
   Given the 7-day baseline stockout risk and fragmented access pathways, establish a national strategic cisplatin reserve (60–90 day buffer) held at a central distribution point with protocols for rapid inter-hospital deployment. This reserve should be indexed to currency fluctuations to maintain purchasing power.

3. **Coordinate Multi-Channel Procurement**  
   Formalize procurement coordination protocols between public hospitals, PAMI, and obras sociales to reduce order fragmentation, improve negotiating leverage with suppliers, and enable rapid reallocation during stock-outs. Current semi-formal coordination mechanisms (drug banks, inter-service loans) should be codified into standing operational procedures.

4. **Currency Risk Hedging**  
   Explore advance purchase agreements or currency-hedged procurement contracts for cisplatin and other critical oncology drugs traded in USD, to partially insulate supply decisions from peso devaluation cycles.

5. **Monitor API Supplier Concentration**  
   Track regulatory and commercial developments at Indian and Chinese cisplatin API manufacturers. Establish early warning protocols linked to ANMAT and international regulatory bodies (FDA, EMA) for supply chain intelligence.

6. **Pre-position Registration Pathways**  
   Maintain active relationships with at least two independent generic cisplatin manufacturers with valid ANMAT registrations. If a single registered manufacturer exits the market, implement expedited registration pathways to activate alternative suppliers before supply channels close.
