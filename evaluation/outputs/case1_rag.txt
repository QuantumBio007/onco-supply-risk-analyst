# Drug Shortage Risk Brief: Cisplatin in Argentina — Baseline Scenario

## Drug Profile

Cisplatin is a platinum-based alkylating chemotherapy agent used to treat testicular, ovarian, bladder, lung, and head and neck cancers. It has been on the WHO Model List of Essential Medicines since 1979 and remains on the current oncology section.

Cisplatin is fully off-patent with multiple generic manufacturers worldwide. **Active Pharmaceutical Ingredient (API) origin:** The overwhelming majority of cisplatin API is manufactured in India and China, which together account for over 80% of global cisplatin API supply. No significant API manufacturing exists in Latin America. Argentina is fully import-dependent.

Cisplatin is available as an injectable solution or powder for reconstitution. A critical storage requirement: cisplatin must be stored at room temperature (15–25°C) and protected from light. Importantly, **cisplatin should NOT be refrigerated**—refrigeration causes precipitation and renders the product unusable. This storage requirement differs from many other oncology injectables and is a material factor in cold-chain logistics planning.

## Supply Chain Vulnerability

**Upstream concentration:** API supply is concentrated in two countries (India, China). Any export restriction, regulatory action, or geopolitical disruption affecting either country simultaneously disrupts all generic manufacturers globally.

**Generic market pressure:** Generic competition reduces manufacturer margins, leading some producers to exit the market. This creates single-source or dual-source API scenarios for specific formulations, limiting substitution flexibility.

**Regional import dependence:** Argentina has no alternative regional API source and is entirely dependent on imports from India and China via global distribution networks.

**Regulatory approval lag:** In Argentina, ANMAT (Administración Nacional de Medicamentos, Alimentos y Tecnología Médica) must approve any new supplier or alternative manufacturer before legal distribution. Standard ANMAT registration timelines are approximately 12–18 months for generic drugs. During normal operations, this is not acutely constraining. However, it becomes a critical bottleneck during global API supply disruptions—even if alternative suppliers exist internationally, they cannot supply Argentina until ANMAT registration is complete. This regulatory lag effectively extends the practical lead time during supply emergencies.

**Fragmented procurement visibility:** Argentina's multi-channel procurement landscape (public hospitals, obras sociales, provincial systems, private sector) creates structural visibility gaps. No single entity has complete, real-time operational visibility into national oncology drug stock across all channels. A critical distinction exists between formal coverage and real access: many treatments are nominally covered by obras sociales, PAMI, or prepagas, but actual patient access depends on clinical authorization, reimbursement processing, supplier contracts, and dispensing logistics. Each step introduces latency and risk of failure independent of aggregate drug availability. The gap between formal coverage and effective access is a structural feature of the Argentine health system, not an exceptional failure mode.

**Historical context:** Cisplatin experienced a documented global supply disruption in 2023–2024 driven by Indian API manufacturing issues and US FDA-forced plant shutdowns. This disruption was superimposed on Argentina's domestic structural environment, creating compounded scarcity.

## Policy Recommendations

1. **Establish national visibility and coordination:** Create a centralized monitoring dashboard (similar to SISCAATS in Colombia) that integrates stockout and inventory data across public hospitals, obras sociales, provincial drug banks, and PAMI. Real-time visibility will enable rapid inter-channel transfer during localized supply tightness and reduce reliance on informal clinician-coordinated logistics.

2. **Implement strategic buffer stock:** Maintain a national strategic cisplatin reserve of 60–90 days of supply (approximately 500–700 units based on modeled mean demand). This buffer should be held in a central warehouse accessible to all procurement channels during emergencies and should include explicit protocols for release authorization.

3. **Coordinate ANMAT pre-approval of alternative suppliers:** Work with ANMAT to pre-qualify and register 2–3 alternative cisplatin suppliers from India and China before any disruption occurs. This de-risks the 12–18 month regulatory lag that would otherwise activate during an API export restriction. Pre-approval costs minimal and is insurance against lead-time extension.

4. **Establish procurement coordination protocols:** Formalize written agreements between ANMAT, Ministerio de Salud, provincial health authorities, obras sociales representatives, and major hospital networks on cisplatin procurement triggers, order quantities, and inventory targets. Include explicit mechanisms for demand forecasting and inter-channel stock transfers during shortage events.

5. **Monitor global API supply signals:** Subscribe to manufacturer alerts, INVIMA/ANMAT regulatory announcements, and Indian/Chinese regulatory actions (e.g., FDA warning letters, EMA compliance inspections). Create a trigger protocol: if any major cisplatin API manufacturer in India or China experiences regulatory action or announces capacity constraints, activate increased national purchasing and buffer stock review.

6. **Embed shortage response in clinical protocols:** Work with oncology societies and hospital networks to document clinical alternatives (e.g., carboplatin substitution, treatment schedule modification) in case of sustained cisplatin shortage. However, note that **carboplatin shares the same India/China API supply chain** — both drugs would be disrupted simultaneously by an API export restriction, eliminating substitution flexibility. Diversification options are limited.

## Confidence & Limitations

**Strengths of this analysis:**
- Baseline parameters (lead time, fill rate, budget constraint) are grounded in documented historical performance during 2023–2024.
- Monte Carlo simulation with 500 runs provides robust confidence intervals.
- Reflects structural features of Argentine procurement (multi-channel fragmentation, budget constraints, regulatory approval timelines) documented by ANMAT and FIFARMA.

**Key limitations:**
- Stockout projections are based on a stylized continuous-review inventory model (Warren OM framework) and are illustrative rather than actuarial. They do not capture all real-world supply chain complexity (e.g., partial shipments, quality rejections, customs delays).
- Demand distribution is modeled as normal (truncated at zero) based on aggregated clinical use. Actual demand varies by hospital, region, and cancer prevalence; the model does not capture intra-year seasonality or rare spike events.
- The 86% fill rate assumption in baseline reflects "normal supplier performance" but is not granular by supplier. If a single supplier experiences a partial outage, actual fill rate may drop below 86% without triggering a formal global shortage declaration.
- Procurement data reflect publicly available sources as of 2024. Real-time inventory levels across all Argentine procurement channels are not publicly reported; modeled "average inventory on hand" is a theoretical construct, not an observed count.
- The analysis does not quantify the impact of fragmented procurement visibility on effective access. A 7-day stockout in a centralized system is operationally manageable; the same 7 days distributed across uncoordinated hospital networks can translate to treatment delays for individual patients if stock is concentrated in the wrong channel.
- Storage logistics (room temperature, light protection) are a known requirement but are not modeled as a failure point. If hospitals or distributors inadvertently refrigerate cisplatin, usable stock can be destroyed without triggering recorded shortages.
- The baseline scenario does not model the impact of currency devaluation or import tariff changes on procurement budget, both relevant to Argentina's macroeconomic volatility.

**Shared API supply chain with carboplatin:** The context confirms that cisplatin and carboplatin share the same API supply chain (India, China as primary sources). An Indian or Chinese API export restriction, manufacturing shutdown, or quality enforcement action would disrupt **both cisplatin and carboplatin simultaneously**, eliminating substitution flexibility for oncology programs that use both platinum agents. This is a material constraint on diversification strategy.
