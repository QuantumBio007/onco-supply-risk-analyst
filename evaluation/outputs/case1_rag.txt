# Drug Shortage Risk Brief: Cisplatin in Argentina (Baseline Scenario)

## Drug Profile

Cisplatin is a platinum-based alkylating chemotherapy agent used to treat testicular, ovarian, bladder, lung, and head and neck cancers. It has been on the WHO Model List of Essential Medicines since 1979 and remains on the current oncology section, signaling that countries should treat it as a procurement priority.

Cisplatin is fully off-patent with multiple generic manufacturers worldwide, creating price competition that lowers manufacturer margins and can drive market consolidation.

**Active Pharmaceutical Ingredient (API) origin:** The overwhelming majority of cisplatin API is manufactured in India and China, which together account for over 80% of global supply. No significant API manufacturing exists in Latin America. Argentina is fully import-dependent.

**Formulation and storage:** Cisplatin is available as an injectable solution or powder for reconstitution. It must be stored at room temperature (15–25°C) and protected from light. Critically, cisplatin should NOT be refrigerated — refrigeration causes precipitation and renders the product unusable.

---

## Supply Chain Vulnerability

**Concentration of API supply:**  
API supply for cisplatin is concentrated in India and China. Any export restriction, regulatory action, or geopolitical disruption simultaneously affects all generic manufacturers globally. No alternative regional API source exists for Latin American importers.

**Shared platinum API chain:**  
Cisplatin and carboplatin share the same API supply chain (both sourced primarily from India, secondarily from China). An Indian or Chinese API export restriction, manufacturing shutdown, or quality enforcement action disrupts both cisplatin and carboplatin simultaneously, eliminating substitution flexibility for oncology programs that use both platinum agents.

**Generic market consolidation risk:**  
Price pressure among generic manufacturers reduces profit margins, leading some producers to exit the market. Single-source or dual-source API scenarios for specific formulations are common. The 2023–2024 global cisplatin/carboplatin shortage (driven by Indian API manufacturing issues and US FDA-forced plant shutdowns) demonstrates this vulnerability.

**Argentine procurement fragmentation:**  
Argentina's multi-channel procurement landscape (public hospitals, obras sociales, private insurers, provincial drug banks) creates a critical information visibility gap. No single entity in Argentina has complete, integrated, real-time operational visibility into national oncology drug stock across all channels. This fragmentation means that:
- A hospital or provincial system may face local shortage while transferable stock exists elsewhere but is unknown.
- Formal coverage (nominally available through obras sociales or public hospitals) does not equal real access; reimbursement processing, supply contracts, and dispensing logistics introduce latency and failure risk at each step.
- Emergency mechanisms (inter-service drug loans, protocol modification, treatment delays) are semi-formal and not scalable substitutes for institutional supply chain reliability.

**Regulatory lag constraint:**  
ANMAT (Administración Nacional de Medicamentos, Alimentos y Tecnología Médica) approval is required for any new supplier or alternative manufacturer. Standard ANMAT registration timelines are 12–18 months for generic drugs. During an API export restriction, this regulatory clearance requirement becomes a critical constraint: even if alternative suppliers exist globally, they cannot supply Argentina until ANMAT registration is complete, effectively extending practical lead time by 6–18 months during supply disruptions.

## Scenario Impact Analysis

**Baseline Scenario (Normal Operations):**

Monte Carlo simulation (500 runs) under normal operations conditions projects:

- **Stockout days per year:** 7.0 ± 0.65 (95% CI)
- **Service level (unit fill rate):** 98.3% ± 0.0% (95% CI)
- **Service level (days without stockout):** 98.1%
- **Probability of any stockout during the year:** 74%
- **Probability of critical shortage (>60 stockout days):** 0.0%
- **Average inventory on hand:** 208 units

**Model assumptions:**
- Mean lead time: 35.0 days (std: 8.8 days)
- Order fill rate: 86% (reflecting normal supplier performance)
- Effective budget: 75% of theoretical optimal
- Safety stock: 117 units; Reorder point: 397 units; Economic order quantity: 348 units

**Interpretation:**

Under baseline conditions, cisplatin supply in Argentina is projected to experience approximately 7 stockout days per year, with a unit service level of 98.3%. This is within acceptable operational range. However, the 74% probability of experiencing *any* stockout during the year, even if brief, indicates that supply tightness is an inherent feature of the current system. The gap between formal coverage and effective access, combined with procurement fragmentation, means that even statistical "acceptable" performance masks real clinical impact in hospitals or patient populations lacking visibility or access to alternative supply channels.

**Clinical context:**  
Cisplatin is a standard component of curative regimens for testicular cancer, ovarian cancer, and head and neck malignancies. Even brief stockouts (7 days average) can delay chemotherapy cycles, potentially compromising oncologic outcomes. The baseline scenario does not account for the structural access delays documented by the FIFARMA/IQVIA Patient W.A.I.T. Indicator 2024, which found that only 35% of internationally approved medicines have full or partial public availability in Argentina's formal health system and that oncology/orphan drug treatments had the worst performance (87% with no progress in access or approval in 2023–2024).

## Policy Recommendations

1. **Establish a national strategic cisplatin reserve:**  
   Maintain a 60–90 day buffer stock of cisplatin at a central location (e.g., BNDE or a designated public hospital network hub) accessible to all provincial systems. This mitigates fragmentation and ensures surge capacity during lead time extensions.

2. **Create an integrated supply visibility system:**  
   Implement a real-time dashboard that aggregates cisplatin inventory data across public hospitals, obras sociales, provincial drug banks, and major private distributors. Link this to ANMAT's existing traceability infrastructure to resolve the current "no single entity has complete visibility" gap.

3. **Pre-register alternative cisplatin suppliers with ANMAT:**  
   Proactively pursue regulatory approval of at least two additional API suppliers (beyond current incumbents) before a crisis occurs. This reduces the 12–18 month lag when export restrictions trigger urgent supplier switching.

4. **Coordinate procurement timing across channels:**  
   Establish a quarterly coordination mechanism (e.g., ANMAT + public procurement + major obras sociales) to synchronize ordering and reduce batch-driven volatility. Fragmented independent ordering amplifies lead time variability.

5. **Develop carboplatin-cisplatin contingency protocols:**  
   Since carboplatin and cisplatin share the same API supply chain, any Indian/Chinese disruption affects both. Work with oncology societies to develop and pre-approve clinical protocols that specify when carboplatin can substitute for cisplatin and vice versa, reducing clinical improvisation during shortages.

6. **Strengthen monitoring of generic manufacturer market:**  
   Track consolidation and exit of cisplatin manufacturers in India and China through trade journals and regulatory announcements. Establish an early-warning mechanism within ANMAT to flag supplier base erosion before it causes supply failure.

7. **Formalize emergency access mechanisms:**  
   Codify the currently semi-formal inter-hospital drug loans, provincial bank access, and expedited reimbursement processes into explicit protocols. Include clinical, pharmacist, and social worker roles. This increases scalability and reduces case-by-case dependency on individual initiative.

## Confidence & Limitations

**Confidence:**
- WHO EML status and off-patent generic status of cisplatin are confirmed in the retrieved context.
- API manufacturing concentration in India and China (>80%) is explicitly documented.
- Shared API supply chain with carboplatin is confirmed.
- Argentine regulatory lag (ANMAT 12–18 month timelines) and absence of regional API manufacturing are documented.
- The structural gap between formal coverage and real access in Argentina is well-documented in the FIFARMA/IQVIA Patient W.A.I.T. Indicator 2024 and the Argentina procurement system analysis.
- Baseline Monte Carlo results (7.0 stockout days/year, 98.3% service level) are derived from a validated continuous-review inventory model with 500 simulations.

**Limitations:**
- **Model scope:** Stockout figures are derived from an inventory optimization model (Warren OM framework) and reflect aggregate demand and lead time assumptions. They do not account for:
  - Episodic surges in demand (e.g., clustering of new cancer diagnoses or protocol changes).
  - Heterogeneous availability across channels (a public hospital may face shortage while a prepaga has stock, and no integration mechanism exists to resolve this).
  - Clinical rationing or protocol substitution not captured in demand data.

- **Data gaps:** The retrieved context does not provide:
  - Granular demand data (monthly/quarterly volumes) for cisplatin in Argentina.
  - Current distributor names, capacities, or fill rates for Argentina-specific suppliers.
  - Real-time ANMAT registration backlog or processing times specific to oncology drugs.
  - Detailed inter-channel stock transfer protocols or costs.

- **Structural access gap not fully modeled:** The analysis accounts for lead time and order fill rate but does not quantify the reimbursement processing lag, clinical authorization delays, or inter-payer coordination friction documented in the Argentina procurement system context. Real-world time-to-patient-access is longer than pharmaceutical "stockout."

- **Baseline assumptions:** The 86% order fill rate and 35-day lead time reflect "normal operations" as of 2024 but may not be durable if generic manufacturer consolidation accelerates or if geopolitical tensions affect India/China trade.

- **Carboplatin co-disruption risk:** The brief identifies the shared platinum API chain but does not model the probabilistic overlap of cisplatin and carboplatin shortages or the clinical impact of losing both agents simultaneously.

- **Regional context:** The FIFARMA 87% no-progress rate for orphan/oncology drugs in 2023–2024 indicates systemic access challenges beyond cisplatin. The baseline model does not capture whether cisplatin demand itself is suppressed due to earlier-stage access barriers (diagnosis, referral) not visible in pharmaceutical dispensing data.
