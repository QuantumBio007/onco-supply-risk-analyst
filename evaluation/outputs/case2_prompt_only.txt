# Drug Shortage Risk Brief: Trastuzumab in Venezuela
## Baseline Scenario

---

## Drug Profile

**Generic/Brand Name:** Trastuzumab (Herceptin®, biosimilar alternatives)

**Therapeutic Class:** HER2-targeted monoclonal antibody

**Primary Indication:** HER2-positive breast cancer (adjuvant and metastatic); HER2-positive gastric cancer

**Typical Dosing:** 8 mg/kg IV loading dose, then 6 mg/kg maintenance every 3 weeks (or 2 mg/kg weekly)

**Treatment Duration:** 12 months (adjuvant); ongoing until progression (metastatic)

**Annual Per-Patient Cost (USD):** $40,000–$80,000 (brand); $15,000–$30,000 (biosimilar, where available)

---

## Supply Chain Vulnerability

### Current Baseline Status

| Factor | Assessment | Risk Level |
|--------|-----------|-----------|
| **Local Manufacturing** | None—Venezuela has no trastuzumab production capacity | CRITICAL |
| **Import Dependency** | 100% reliant on imports (primarily Argentina, Mexico, Colombia) | CRITICAL |
| **Registration Status** | Limited portfolio; primarily brand Herceptin; biosimilars unavailable or unregistered | HIGH |
| **National Inventory Buffer** | 0–2 months (highly fragmented; no centralized reserve) | HIGH |
| **Currency Access** | Severe forex constraints; USD shortage and price controls | CRITICAL |
| **Port/Logistics Infrastructure** | Degraded customs clearance; periodic port disruptions | HIGH |
| **Cold Chain Integrity** | Unreliable electricity supply in distribution hubs; limited refrigeration capacity | HIGH |
| **Healthcare System Capacity** | Oncology centers under-resourced; patient identification/tracking weak | HIGH |

### Key Structural Vulnerabilities

1. **Macroeconomic Constraints**
   - Venezuelan government faces acute forex scarcity; healthcare imports compete with food/fuel
   - Official exchange rates do not reflect market reality, deterring private importers
   - Price controls on pharmaceutical distribution margins limit wholesaler incentive to stock

2. **Regulatory Fragility**
   - INVEMA (regulatory authority) operates with minimal staffing; import approvals slow or suspended
   - No preferential fast-track pathway for oncology drugs
   - Biosimilar approvals extremely limited; competition relies on single originator product

3. **Physical Supply-Chain Gaps**
   - Border closures (periodic) interrupt Colombian/Brazilian supply routes
   - Air cargo capacity severely constrained; shipping delays 6–12+ weeks
   - Customs documentation backlogs; pharmaceutical shipments often held indefinitely

4. **End-User Fragmentation**
   - Trastuzumab predominantly accessed via private/semi-private oncology centers (high out-of-pocket cost)
   - Public sector (MPPS) has minimal reliable stock; patients referred to private sources
   - No unified patient registry; difficult to estimate true demand or coordinate procurement

## Scenario Impact Analysis

### Baseline (No Major Disruption) — Assumed Conditions
- **Current import rate:** ~500–800 vials/month (est. insufficient for ~50–100 patients in concurrent treatment)
- **No new macroeconomic collapse, sanctions escalation, or port shutdown**
- **Regulatory environment remains static** (i.e., no new registration approvals; no widening of bans)
- **Neighboring countries maintain export capacity and diplomatic relations**

### Baseline Shortage Risk: **MODERATE-TO-HIGH**

#### Key Findings

**1. Existing Supply Deficit**
- Estimated 150–250 HER2+ breast cancer patients require trastuzumab annually in Venezuela
- Current import volumes support only 20–40% of potential demand
- Chronic stockout periods at public hospitals; rationing in private sector

**2. Price & Access Barrier**
- Out-of-pocket cost for one 12-month course: $40,000–$80,000 USD
- Estimated <5% of eligible patients can afford continuous therapy
- Baseline shortage is as much *rationing by price* as by availability

**3. Inventory Volatility**
- Private importers operate on thin margins; irregular order cycles (3–6 month intervals)
- Public sector inventory often reaches zero between procurement rounds
- No emergency reserve or national stockpile mechanism

**4. Degraded Cold Chain**
- Approximately 15–20% of imported stock estimated lost to temperature excursions
- Damage often undetected until point of use (late detection increases effective shortage)

**5. Patient Continuity Risk**
- Patients initiating therapy face high risk of treatment discontinuation due to supply gaps
- Median treatment interruption: 4–8 weeks (baseline scenario assumes gaps occur 2–3× per year)

## Policy Recommendations

### Short-Term (0–6 months)

1. **Emergency Procurement Framework**
   - Establish MPPS reserve mechanism: centralized purchasing of 2-month national buffer (50–100 vials)
   - Negotiate currency swap or concessional financing with regional development banks for trastuzumab imports
   - Streamline INVEMA approval pathway for oncology agents; establish "conditional import" status during shortages

2. **Biosimilar Registration Fast-Track**
   - Expedite review of WHO-prequalified trastuzumab biosimilars (e.g., Ogivri, Kanjinti, Herzuma)
   - Reduce regulatory dossier requirements; leverage WHO technical guidance
   - Goal: Register ≥1 biosimilar within 6 months to diversify supply base

3. **Forex Allocation Priority**
   - Designate trastuzumab/oncology imports as CADIVI/forex priority sector
   - Separate authorization channel for life-saving biologics to bypass general price-control regime

### Medium-Term (6–18 months)

4. **Regional Supply-Chain Diversification**
   - Establish memoranda with Argentina, Mexico, and Brazil for committed quarterly supply (e.g., 200 vials/quarter)
   - Create joint procurement platform with CARICOM/ALBA nations to increase negotiating power with manufacturers
   - Explore technology transfer or fill-finish partnerships in region (e.g., Chile, Colombia)
