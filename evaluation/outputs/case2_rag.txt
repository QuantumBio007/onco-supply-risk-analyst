# Drug Shortage Risk Brief: Trastuzumab in Venezuela — Baseline Scenario

## Drug Profile

Trastuzumab is a monoclonal antibody targeting the HER2 receptor, used to treat HER2-positive breast cancer and HER2-positive gastric cancer. It was originally developed by Genentech (marketed as Herceptin by Roche).

**WHO Essential Medicines Status:** Trastuzumab was added to the WHO Model List of Essential Medicines core list in 2019 (first appeared on complementary list in 2015), confirming its designation as a globally essential medicine regardless of cost.

**Manufacturing Origin & Supply Chain Characteristics:** Unlike small-molecule generics (e.g., cisplatin, doxorubicin), trastuzumab is a biologic manufactured through cell culture processes and cannot be synthesized from chemical precursors. The manufacturing base is concentrated among a small number of specialized biologic facilities globally. There is no India/China generic API dynamic for this drug. Multiple biosimilars are approved globally (Herzuma, Ogivri, Kanjinti, and others); however, biosimilar uptake in Latin America is slower than in high-income countries due to regulatory complexity, physician familiarity, and procurement inertia.

**Unit Cost:** A full treatment course of originator trastuzumab can cost USD 50,000–70,000 per patient per year. Even biosimilars are substantially more expensive than generic small-molecule chemotherapy, making budget pressure the primary driver of access failures.

**Cold Chain Requirement:** Trastuzumab must be refrigerated at 2–8°C throughout the entire supply chain from manufacture to pharmacy to patient. Any break in the cold chain compromises product integrity. This makes trastuzumab uniquely vulnerable to logistics disruptions in Venezuela, where infrastructure variability is high.

## Supply Chain Vulnerability

**Venezuela's Structural Oncology Crisis:**

According to the Lancet Oncology (2017), Venezuela had available only approximately 10% of the medicines needed for cancer care. This is independently confirmed by Duma & Duque Duran (Journal of Global Oncology 2019), documenting chronic unavailability of cisplatin, bevacizumab, aromatase inhibitors, and tamoxifen across Venezuelan public hospitals.

**Trastuzumab-Specific Availability:** Trastuzumab is unavailable in the public sector for most patients throughout 2020–2025 and is accessible only via private dollarized purchase or NGO emergency supply. Approximately 4,000 pediatric oncology patients per year contact NGO networks for medication, with fewer than 10% obtaining oncology drugs through formal channels (Global Cancer Institute reporting).

**Chronic Fiscal Constraints:**

As of September 2025, El Nacional reported "massive debt with suppliers and closure of international credit lines" resulting from chronic foreign-currency shortage for sanitary imports. The Venezuelan pharmaceutical sector was operating at only 38.4% of installed capacity in Q3 2023 (Conindustria/Cifar).

**Current Medicine Shortage Prevalence:**

- Convite's March 2024 monthly monitoring bulletin: 28.4% overall medicine shortage
- Encuesta Nacional de Hospitales (ENH, September 2024): 37.4% medicine shortage in public health centers
- Historical peak (2018–2019): 85–95% hospital-level shortages
- Partial recovery to 28–37% shortage rates since 2022–2024 reflects normalization of a dollarized private economy rather than restoration of public procurement capacity

**Procurement System Vulnerabilities Specific to Trastuzumab:**

1. **Cold chain infrastructure fragility:** Port delays without adequate refrigeration, customs holds without cold storage, or power outages at warehouses can render entire shipments unusable. Venezuela's infrastructure variability amplifies this risk compared to high-income countries.

2. **High cost and budget scarcity:** Budget pressure — driven by currency devaluation and fiscal austerity — affects trastuzumab access first and most severely among oncology drugs due to its unit cost (50–70 times that of generic chemotherapy).

3. **Biologic manufacturing concentration:** Unlike generic chemotherapy drugs, there is no substitutable manufacturing base. Supply is concentrated in a small number of global biologic facilities, all of which require currency for import.

4. **Informal supply channel dependence:** With public-sector access effectively unavailable, private dollarized purchase and NGO supply are the primary access routes for patients not covered by private insurance.

The baseline scenario models trastuzumab supply in Venezuela under chronic structural constraints — not an acute shock scenario. **Clinical Implications:**

With trastuzumab unavailable ~51% of the year and available at only 38% of required capacity when not in stockout, patients cannot access continuous, guideline-concordant HER2-targeted therapy. This is incompatible with the standard treatment protocol for HER2-positive breast cancer, which requires sustained exposure over 12 months.

## Policy Recommendations

**Note:** The following recommendations address the structural barriers to trastuzumab access in Venezuela. However, they assume restoration of baseline state capacity (currency, fiscal stability, central procurement coordination), which is not present in the baseline scenario modeled above.

1. **Establish a multilateral supply agreement for trastuzumab biosimilars:**
   - Coordinate with PAHO and bilateral donors (e.g., Brazil, Colombia, ECLAC) to negotiate tiered-pricing agreements for WHO-approved trastuzumab biosimilars.
   - Prioritize biosimilar products that can be stored at standard refrigeration (2–8°C) and transported through PAHO's existing oncology distribution network.

2. **Create a dedicated HER2-positive cancer treatment fund:**
   - Allocate hard-currency reserves specifically for trastuzumab procurement, separate from general medicine budgets. Current evidence shows budget constraints drive trastuzumab access failure first among oncology drugs.
   - Link procurement to disease registry data (HER2-positive cases) to establish evidence-based demand.

3. **Strengthen cold chain infrastructure:**
   - Prioritize refrigerated container capacity at port entry points and regional warehouses to prevent temperature-dependent product loss.
   - Establish backup power and temperature monitoring at central pharmaceutical depots.

4. **Formalize NGO and private-sector supply channels for transparency:**
   - Since 4,000 pediatric oncology patients annually contact NGOs (with <10% obtaining drugs through formal channels), regularize NGO access to approved biosimilars at cost-recovery prices to bridge the public-sector availability gap.

5. **Report trastuzumab shortage to WHO medicines shortage platform:**
   - As a WHO EML-listed drug, Venezuela should formally report trastuzumab shortage status to activate WHO technical assistance and donor coordination mechanisms.
   - EML inclusion means trastuzumab shortage is not merely a supply chain problem but a documented failure of the health system's obligation to provide essential care.

## Confidence & Limitations

**Confidence:**

- **High confidence** in the characterization of Venezuela's chronic oncology drug shortage: multiple independent sources (Lancet Oncology 2017, Duma & Duque Duran 2019, Convite 2024, ENH September 2024, Global Cancer Institute) confirm ~10% oncology drug availability and 28–37% overall medicine shortage.
- **High confidence** in trastuzumab's WHO EML status and manufacturing characteristics: sourced directly from WHO EML documentation and verified biologic manufacturing literature.
- **High confidence** in trastuzumab's accessibility pattern in Venezuela (private dollarized purchase, NGO supply, public-sector unavailability): documented in Global Cancer Institute reporting and consistent with Venezuela's broader health-sector collapse.

**Limitations:**

- **Stockout projections (185.4 days/year) are illustrative outputs from a simplified (Q, r) inventory model**, not actuarial forecasts. The model assumes:
  - Demand follows a Poisson distribution (appropriate for chronic disease therapy demand but potentially unstable under severe supply constraints)
  - Lead times are log-normally distributed with mean 60 days — a simplified estimate based on Venezuelan import disruption patterns, not real-time procurement data
  - No recovery phase is modeled; the baseline assumes disruption persists for the full year, representing a worst-case bound
  - The 38% fill rate and 20% budget multiplier are structural parameters derived from documented failure rates, but actual order-by-order outcomes vary

- **No direct patient-level data:** The brief does not include actual pharmaceutical transaction data from Venezuelan hospitals, public clinics, or private insurance systems. All oncology-specific claims rely on survey-based and NGO reports.

- **Cold chain loss is not quantified:** While the context notes that temperature excursions can render shipments unusable and that this risk is "higher in Latin America," the baseline model does not separate temperature-related loss from demand/budget-driven shortage. Actual
