# Drug Shortage Risk Brief: Trastuzumab in Venezuela — Baseline Scenario

## Drug Profile

Trastuzumab is a monoclonal antibody targeting the HER2 receptor, used to treat HER2-positive breast cancer and HER2-positive gastric cancer. It was developed by Genentech and marketed by Roche as Herceptin.

**WHO Essential Medicines Status:** Trastuzumab was added to the WHO Model List of Essential Medicines in 2015 (complementary list) and transferred to the core list in 2019. This designation establishes it as a globally essential medicine, creating formal procurement obligations for member states regardless of cost.

**Manufacturing Origin & Supply Chain Structure:** Unlike small-molecule chemotherapy agents, trastuzumab is a biologic manufactured through cell culture processes and cannot be synthesized from chemical precursors. Manufacturing is concentrated among a small number of specialized biologic facilities globally. There is no India/China generic API dynamic for this drug — the manufacturing base is more concentrated and specialized than for conventional chemotherapy. Multiple biosimilars are approved globally (Herzuma, Ogivri, Kanjinti), but uptake in Latin America is slower than in high-income countries due to regulatory complexity, physician familiarity, and procurement inertia.

**Cold Chain Requirement:** Trastuzumab must be refrigerated at 2–8°C throughout the entire supply chain from manufacture to pharmacy to patient. Any break in the cold chain compromises product integrity, making the drug uniquely vulnerable to logistics disruptions — port delays without adequate refrigeration, customs holds without cold storage, or warehouse power outages can render entire shipments unusable. This infrastructure vulnerability is particularly acute in Venezuela.

**Cost & Budget Impact:** A full course of originator trastuzumab (Herceptin) costs USD 50,000–70,000 per patient per year. Even biosimilars cost substantially more than generic small-molecule chemotherapy. Budget pressure from currency devaluation and fiscal austerity affects trastuzumab access first and most severely.

## Supply Chain Vulnerability

**Structural Procurement Crisis in Venezuela:**  
Venezuela's oncology drug procurement system is in systemic failure. The Lancet Oncology (2017) documented that only approximately 10% of medicines needed for cancer care are available. Trastuzumab is unavailable in the public sector for most patients throughout 2020–2025 and is accessible only via private dollarized purchase or NGO emergency supply.

As of September 2025, Venezuela faces "massive debt with suppliers and closure of international credit lines" resulting from chronic foreign-currency shortage for sanitary imports. The pharmaceutical sector was operating at only 38.4% of installed capacity in Q3 2023.

**Baseline Procurement Metrics:**  
Under baseline (normal operations) conditions:
- **Order fill rate:** 57% (structural floor of 60% × scenario multiplier of 95%)
- **Effective budget multiplier:** 30% (reflecting chronic fiscal constraints independent of scenario shock)
- **Mean lead time:** 60 days (standard deviation: 36 days, log-normal distribution)

The 57% fill rate reflects chronic structural procurement failures — approximately 40% of orders fail or arrive severely short due to fiscal constraints and reliance on informal supply chains, not scenario-specific shocks.

**Cold Chain Infrastructure Risk:**  
Venezuela's degraded infrastructure creates compounding vulnerability. Port facilities lack adequate refrigeration; customs holds occur without cold storage; warehouse power outages are routine. These conditions are particularly dangerous for a heat-sensitive biologic like trastuzumab and can render entire shipments unusable.

**Concentrated Manufacturing Base:**  
Unlike cisplatin or doxorubicin (which can be sourced from multiple API suppliers in India and China), trastuzumab's concentrated biologic manufacturing creates a more brittle supply chain with fewer geographic alternatives.

## Policy Recommendations

Given that trastuzumab is on the WHO Essential Medicines core list (as of 2019), Venezuela's baseline shortage constitutes a failure of the health system's obligation to provide essential cancer care. The following recommendations address both the structural constraints and immediate access gaps:

1. **Establish Emergency International Procurement Mechanism**  
   Venezuela's chronic foreign-currency shortage and supplier debt make unilateral procurement impossible. Regional mechanisms (PAHO, ALBA, bilateral agreements with high-capacity jurisdictions like Argentina or Colombia) or international NGO procurement partnerships (e.g., UNITAID, direct manufacturer donated supplies) are necessary to bridge the gap between baseline fiscal capacity (30% effective budget) and actual need.

2. **Create Cold Chain Contingency Protocol**  
   Given Venezuela's warehouse and port infrastructure vulnerabilities, establish dedicated refrigerated containers or partnerships with cold-chain logistics providers for all trastuzumab shipments. Any shipment arriving without cold-chain verification should be quarantined pending integrity testing rather than presumed usable.

3. **Implement Centralized Inventory Visibility System**  
   Venezuela's public hospital system lacks unified stock tracking. A centralized (or region-coordinated) inventory dashboard for trastuzumab would enable rapid reallocation from low-census to high-census hospitals and reduce effective stockouts through internal redistribution.

4. **Prioritize Biosimilar Registration and Uptake**  
   Multiple trastuzumab biosimilars (Herzuma, Ogivri, Kanjinti) are available globally at lower cost than originator Herceptin. Expedited INVEFA (Venezuelan regulatory authority) review and EPS/insurance formulary inclusion of pre-approved biosimilars could reduce per-unit cost and increase accessible supply within budget constraints.

5. **Establish NGO-Public Sector Coordination Framework**  
   Approximately 4,000 pediatric oncology patients per year are already accessing supply through NGO networks. Formalize public sector coordination with these organizations to ensure NGO supply supplements (rather than replaces) public procurement and prevents duplicate gaps.

6. **Document and Report Shortage to WHO**  
   As trastuzumab is on the WHO EML core list, Venezuela should report this baseline shortage through WHO's medicines shortage reporting platform. This triggers higher-level international attention and may unlock emergency procurement mechanisms.

## Confidence & Limitations

**Model Confidence:**  
The baseline Monte Carlo simulation (500 runs, Poisson demand, log-normal lead times) reflects the Warren Operations Management framework and is internally consistent. The 57% fill rate and 30% budget multiplier are grounded in documented Venezuelan procurement constraints (El Nacional September 2025, MINSALUD reporting, Convite March 2024 monitoring). Stockout figures (79.3 ± 1.2 days) have narrow confidence intervals, indicating stable model behavior.

**Critical Limitations:**

- **No Ground Truth Verification:** The retrieved context does not contain real-time inventory or shipment data from Venezuelan public hospitals, NGO warehouses, or private providers. The 79.3 stockout days figure is a model output, not an observed metric.

- **Cold Chain Impact Unquantified:** While the context identifies cold-chain breakage as a major vulnerability in Venezuela, the model does not separately parameterize the probability or frequency of cold-chain failures. Actual stockout days may exceed the model prediction if cold-chain losses are frequent.

- **Informal Market Opacity:** Venezuela's pharmaceutical supply is increasingly informal and dollarized. The model captures the public procurement system but not the private/NGO supply streams that currently serve most cancer patients. A patient-level shortage assessment would require visibility into private and NGO inventory, which is not available.

- **Structural vs. Shock Uncertainty:** The model designates the 57% fill rate and 30% budget as "structural floor" independent of scenario. However, the distinction between baseline and shock scenarios is uncertain — Venezuela's baseline conditions are already in acute crisis, and the true floor capacity under further stress is not documented.

- **Lead Time Distribution:** The log-normal lead time (mean 60 days, std 36 days) is derived from supply chain theory, not from observed Venezuelan import data. Actual lead times depend on unpredictable port congestion, customs delays, and international shipping disruptions, which vary widely.

- **Demand Assumption:** The model assumes Poisson-distributed demand (integer non-negative). In reality, demand for trastuzumab is non-stationary (driven by new HER2+ diagnoses, treatment protocols, and available budget), and demand may be censored by supply constraints — patients not starting treatment because supply is unavailable do not generate demand signal.
