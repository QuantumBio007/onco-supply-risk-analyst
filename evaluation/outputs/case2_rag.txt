## Drug Profile

Trastuzumab is a monoclonal antibody targeting the HER2 receptor, used to treat HER2-positive breast cancer and HER2-positive gastric cancers. It was added to the WHO Model List of Essential Medicines core list in 2019, confirming its status as a globally essential medicine. 

Unlike small-molecule generics (e.g., cisplatin, carboplatin), trastuzumab is a biologic manufactured through specialized cell-culture processes. The manufacturing base is concentrated among a small number of global biologic facilities; there is no distributed generic API supply chain comparable to India/China cisplatin production. Multiple biosimilars are approved globally (Herzuma, Ogivri, Kanjinti), but biosimilar uptake in Latin America is slower than in high-income countries due to regulatory complexity and procurement inertia.

Trastuzumab requires strict cold-chain maintenance (2–8°C) throughout the entire supply chain from manufacture to patient administration. Any break in the cold chain — port delays without refrigeration, customs holds, or warehouse power outages — renders shipments unusable.

## Supply Chain Vulnerability

Venezuela's oncology drug procurement operates as a residual institutional structure, not a functioning procurement channel. The MPPS (Ministerio del Poder Popular para la Salud) nominally directs procurement, and INHRR (Instituto Nacional de Higiene "Rafael Rangel") is the drug regulatory authority. However, the formal public procurement system is severely constrained by:

- **Foreign-currency collapse**: Chronic inability to pay international suppliers has effectively excluded Venezuela from normal import channels for high-cost biologics.
- **Regulatory bottlenecks**: INHRR registration timelines for biologics historically require 24–48 months; the 2023 SIVERC platform acceleration has not yet offset the backlog. Registrations are country-specific and do not transfer from ANMAT (Argentina) or INVIMA (Colombia).
- **Cold-chain infrastructure**: Venezuela's power grid instability and warehouse refrigeration capacity are inadequate for biologics requiring continuous 2–8°C storage. Port delays and customs holds without temperature-controlled facilities compound this risk.
- **No regional alternative suppliers**: Trastuzumab biosimilars are manufactured by specialized facilities globally; Latin America has no competing biologic manufacturing base.

In practice, documented trastuzumab access in Venezuela relies primarily on NGO programs, diaspora procurement, and dollarized private purchase — not MPPS formal channels. The formal system functions only residually for this high-cost biologic.

## Scenario Impact Analysis

Under baseline conditions (normal international supply availability, no API export restrictions, no additional geopolitical shocks), modeled service levels for trastuzumab in Venezuela are **projected at 60.4% (95% CI: ±2.01%)**, with **74.6 stockout days per year (95% CI: ±2.87 days)**.

This represents **HIGH supply risk**. The baseline already reflects:
- 30% effective procurement budget (structural consequence of fiscal collapse, not a scenario shock)
- 60% structural fill rate (reflects chronic payment arrears and supply allocation constraints)
- 10-day initial stock (consistent with collapsed formal procurement volumes)

**Critical interpretation**: Venezuela's baseline is already a sustained crisis state. The 74.6 stockout days/year does not represent a failure of an otherwise functioning system; it reflects the system operating at its structural floor. Incremental scenario shocks (API export restriction, currency devaluation) would add marginally to this baseline because the system has limited capacity to absorb additional disruption — it is already near collapse.

## Policy Recommendations

1. **Bypass formal procurement bottlenecks**: Given INHRR registration backlogs (18–36+ months for generics; 24–48 months historically for biologics), accelerate use of INHRR's special authorization mechanism for non-registered trastuzumab biosimilars valid for up to 6 months per authorization. This is the primary legal route for innovative oncology drugs outside the standard registration pathway.

2. **Establish NGO-facilitated supply agreements**: Formalize partnerships with international NGOs and diaspora networks that currently supply trastuzumab outside the formal MPPS channel. Document and legalize these flows rather than treating them as informal workarounds.

3. **Cold-chain capacity assessment and investment**: Conduct urgent audit of warehouse refrigeration and port cold-chain infrastructure. Without guaranteed 2–8°C maintenance, even increased procurement volumes will be lost to product spoilage.

4. **Regional biosimilar pricing coordination**: Collaborate with PAHO to explore whether pooled procurement of trastuzumab biosimilars (rather than originator Herceptin) could reduce unit cost and improve access, conditional on INHRR regulatory acceptance of WHO-prequalified biosimilar alternatives.

5. **Transparent inventory visibility**: Establish real-time tracking of trastuzumab stock across MPPS and NGO-supplied channels to prevent coordinated stockouts across parallel supply routes.

## Confidence & Limitations

**Limitations:**
- The stockout projection of 74.6 days/year is derived from a simplified inventory simulation model (not actuarial data) and reflects assumed parameter values (30% budget, 60% fill rate, 10-day initial stock) that are illustrative of documented structural constraints, not measured real-time procurement data.
- Actual trastuzumab consumption and stock levels in Venezuela are not publicly available; NGO and informal supply volumes are not systematically tracked, so the true baseline procurement landscape is partially opaque.
- INHRR registration status and timelines for specific trastuzumab biosimilars are not detailed in the retrieved context; regulatory acceleration under SIVERC (launched March 2023) is undocumented.
- Cold-chain failure rates during port transit and warehouse storage are not quantified in the retrieved context.

**What is more certain:**
- Trastuzumab's WHO EML core-list status (2019) creates international procurement obligations, but Venezuela's fiscal crisis makes these obligations unenforceable through normal channels.
- The biologic manufacturing base is concentrated globally with no Latin American alternative source.
- Formal MPPS procurement for high-cost biologics is severely constrained by foreign-currency shortages and debt with international suppliers, as documented in the source material.
