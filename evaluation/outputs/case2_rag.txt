## Drug Profile

Trastuzumab is a HER2-targeted monoclonal antibody biologic on the WHO Model List of Essential Medicines. It is patent-protected (originator: Herceptin, USD 50,000–70,000 per patient per year) with limited biosimilar availability in Latin America. Trastuzumab requires unbroken cold-chain maintenance throughout the supply chain; any break in refrigeration (port delays, customs holds, warehouse power outages) renders entire shipments unusable. This cold-chain vulnerability is particularly acute in Venezuela, where 66% of the largest hospitals lack consistent running water and documented power instability is endemic.

## Supply Chain Vulnerability

Venezuela's trastuzumab supply chain operates under structural collapse, not temporary disruption:

- **Import dependence:** 100% for trastuzumab and all specialized biologics.
- **Regulatory barrier:** INHRR registration timelines are 12–18 months or longer; registrations are country-specific and do not transfer from other regulatory approvals.
- **Currency control:** Hard-currency allocation for USD-denominated imports is controlled by the national government through successive currency control systems (CADIVI, CENCOEX, DICOM). This is a permanent structural barrier independent of any scenario shock.
- **Procurement channels:** Trastuzumab is accessed through (1) private, dollarized out-of-pocket purchase (affordable only to a small fraction); (2) international NGO and donation programs; and (3) the severely degraded IVSS (social security system).
- **Healthcare financing collapse:** Out-of-pocket health expenditure reached 62.99% of total health spending in 2017 (vs. ~28% regional average). Public healthcare spending fell from 8.1% of GDP in 2006 to 1.18% in 2017.
- **Cold-chain infrastructure failure:** 66% of largest hospitals lack consistent running water; power instability compromises refrigeration for biologics throughout logistics and storage.

**Overall Risk Assessment: CRITICAL**

Under baseline conditions, trastuzumab supply in Venezuela experiences **117.3 stockout days per year**—equivalent to **68% of the year without reliable access**. This reflects:

- Effective order fill rate of 57% (structural floor 60% × scenario 95%), driven by chronic procurement failures due to fiscal constraints and informal supply chains.
- Effective budget multiplier of 30%, reflecting the structural ceiling on foreign-exchange allocation and institutional purchasing power.
- Mean lead time of 60 days (std: 36 days, log-normal), a consequence of port delays, customs holds, and cold-chain infrastructure constraints.
- Permanent disruption duration modeled across the full simulation horizon—no recovery assumption because the structural barriers are persistent.

**Key interpretation:** Venezuela's baseline is already a sustained crisis. The 117.3 stockout days reflect not temporary shocks but endemic system failure. Unlike Argentina (5.1 baseline stockout days) or Colombia (presumed functional baseline), Venezuela lacks a functioning institutional procurement channel for oncology biologics. The simulation models this as a structural supply ceiling, not a scenario deviation.

## Policy Recommendations

The context provided does not contain explicit Venezuelan policy recommendations. However, structural realities warrant consideration:

1. **International coordination:** Strengthen partnerships with international NGO supply programs and donation channels, as these represent the only reliable access pathways outside private out-of-pocket purchase.

2. **Cold-chain rehabilitation:** Any procurement increase depends on urgent infrastructure investment in hospital refrigeration, backup power, and water supply. Without this, shortages persist even if hard currency becomes available.

3. **INHRR expedited pathways:** Explore regulatory streamlining for biosimilar registration (e.g., recognition of WHO prequalification) to reduce 12–18 month approval barriers and increase generic/biosimilar competition.

4. **Transparent reporting:** Formalize shortage documentation to WHO EML reporting platform. Trastuzumab shortage in Venezuela is not a supply chain optimization problem—it is a failure of essential care obligations and warrants international health system attention.

5. **Acknowledgment of limits:** Current policy capacity may not support demand-side interventions (pricing, negotiation) until structural currency and fiscal constraints are addressed at macroeconomic level.

## Confidence & Limitations

**Confidence:**
- Simulation results (117.3 stockout days/year) are derived from an explicit Monte Carlo model with stated parameter assumptions.
- Structural barriers (import dependence, currency controls, INHRR registration timelines, cold-chain infrastructure) are documented in the retrieved context.
- The characterization of Venezuela as a "sustained crisis" baseline is consistent with reported healthcare collapse (1.18% GDP health spending, 72% population unable to access public services as of 2023).

**Limitations:**
- The retrieved context does not contain **primary procurement data** from Venezuelan hospitals, IVSS, or importers. Stockout figures are model-based, not observational.
- The 57% order fill rate and 30% budget multiplier are illustrative structural assumptions, not validated against recent Venezuelan procurement records.
- **Cold-chain failure rates** are not quantified in the context; the 66% hospital water-access statistic is indirect evidence.
- The context does not specify which trastuzumab presentations (IV, SC) or manufacturing origins (originator vs. biosimilar) are relevant to Venezuelan supply.
- **Donation and NGO supply volumes** are not characterized; actual patient access may be higher than the institutional procurement model suggests.
- **Pricing and insurance coverage rules** for trastuzumab in the IVSS and private market are not detailed.

**Data gaps for full risk assessment:**
- Recent (2024–2025) documented trastuzumab stockout events in Venezuela.
- Current hard-currency allocation
