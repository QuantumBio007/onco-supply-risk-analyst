Argentina's oncology drug procurement system is highly fragmented across seven distinct channels that operate independently, without central coordination or shared inventory visibility. This structural fragmentation is the primary institutional driver of drug shortage risk — even when total national supply is adequate, individual channels can run out while others hold surplus, and there is no mechanism to redirect stock across channel boundaries in real time.

The seven procurement channels are: (1) The national and provincial public systems, which procure through centralized tenders and serve patients without formal employment or insurance. Procurement budgets are set in Argentine pesos while drugs are priced in USD, creating chronic currency exposure. (2) Public hospitals, which operate within the public system but manage their own pharmacy stock independently and are the primary point of dispensing for uninsured patients. (3) Obras sociales nacionales — Argentina's national social health insurance funds, tied to union membership and formal employment. There are over 300 obras sociales nacionales, each negotiating its own drug contracts independently. Financial health varies widely: smaller union-based funds are fragile and defer purchases when the exchange rate deteriorates. (4) Obras sociales provinciales, such as IOMA (Instituto de Obra Médico Asistencial) in the Province of Buenos Aires. These are large, institutionally distinct actors with their own procurement logic, formularies, and budget constraints — not interchangeable with national obras sociales. (5) PAMI (Programa de Atención Médica Integral), the national social insurance program for retirees and pensioners. PAMI is one of the largest single purchasers of oncology drugs in Argentina and must be treated as a standalone channel — its formulary decisions and procurement timing have systemic effects on national drug availability. (6) Empresas de medicina prepaga — private health insurance companies such as Swiss Medical. OSDE occupies a hybrid position within the regulatory framework. Prepagas serve a higher-income segment and have somewhat more budget flexibility, but are a minority share of total oncology drug consumption. (7) Private out-of-pocket purchases through pharmacies, representing the smallest share of oncology consumption.

A critical distinction in the Argentine system is between formal coverage and real access. Many oncology treatments are nominally covered by obras sociales, PAMI, or prepagas. However, actual patient access depends on a downstream chain: clinical authorization, processing of the reimbursement file (expediente), contract with the supplier, dispensing logistics, and effective physical delivery. Each step introduces latency and risk of failure that is entirely independent of whether a drug is "available" in the system in aggregate. The gap between formal coverage and effective access is a structural feature of the Argentine health system, not an exceptional failure mode. Supply chain models that treat formal coverage as equivalent to access will systematically underestimate shortage risk.

No single entity in Argentina has complete, integrated, real-time operational visibility into national oncology drug stock across all channels. Regulatory traceability systems exist and allow reconstruction of unit movements, but traceability is not equivalent to a national inventory dashboard. No public body can currently determine, in operational time, which hospital or payer holds transferable available stock to resolve a specific therapeutic emergency. The distinction between traceability and actionable inventory visibility is essential for any shortage risk model.

ANMAT (Administración Nacional de Medicamentos, Alimentos y Tecnología Médica) is Argentina's drug regulatory authority. No drug may be commercially distributed through standard channels without a valid ANMAT registration. If the only registered manufacturer of a specific formulation exits the market, the standard supply pathway is interrupted until another manufacturer completes the registration process — which can take months to years. ANMAT does maintain exceptional importation and compassionate access mechanisms for individual patients, but these do not function as operational replacements for population-level supply and cannot address systemic shortages at scale.

Currency risk operates as a constant background pressure on all import-dependent channels. Oncology drugs are traded in USD globally. Argentina's procurement budgets — public hospitals, obras sociales, and PAMI — are denominated in Argentine pesos. Each devaluation episode simultaneously reduces purchasing power across the entire system. The public channel and smaller obras sociales are most exposed; PAMI's scale provides some negotiating leverage but it remains peso-constrained. Suppliers facing delayed payments reduce credit extension to Argentine buyers, compressing distributor inventory levels downstream.

When a public hospital runs out of a critical oncology drug such as cisplatin, the patient is not automatically redirected to an obra social or prepaga channel: patients without coverage cannot access those channels. The typical sequence involves attempting to source the drug within the same hospital or provincial network; consulting the provincial or national drug bank (banco de drogas); temporarily modifying the treatment protocol if a clinically acceptable alternative exists; delaying the treatment cycle until medication is obtained; or transferring the patient to another public hospital with confirmed availability. In the most severe cases, families, physicians, or hospital administrators pursue administrative escalation or judicial action (judicialización) to force procurement. If these mechanisms fail in time, the real-world consequence is treatment interruption or delay — the most clinically serious outcome of supply chain failure in oncology.

Emergency mechanisms that partially sustain continuity include direct purchases outside normal tender cycles, inter-service drug loans within hospital networks, coordination with provincial and national drug banks, and informal logistics coordinated by clinicians, hospital pharmacists, social workers, and patients' families. These semi-formal mechanisms resolve many individual cases but are not scalable, not systematically tracked, and not a substitute for institutional supply chain reliability.

Fragmentation and limited interoperability across channels may create information asymmetries, weak accountability, and opportunities for inefficient allocation or non-transparent redistribution of scarce medicines — structural vulnerabilities widely discussed within the sector.
