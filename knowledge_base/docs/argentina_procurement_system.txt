Argentina's oncology drug procurement system is highly fragmented across eight distinct channels that operate independently, without central coordination or shared inventory visibility. This structural fragmentation is the primary institutional driver of drug shortage risk — even when total national supply is adequate, individual channels can run out while others hold surplus, and there is no mechanism to redirect stock across channel boundaries in real time. In practice, a ninth mechanism — judicial access via amparo de salud — functions as a de facto parallel channel for patients who cannot obtain drugs through any of the eight formal routes.

THE EIGHT PROCUREMENT CHANNELS

(1) The national and provincial public systems procure through centralized licitaciones públicas (public tenders) and serve patients without formal employment or insurance. Procurement budgets are set in Argentine pesos while drugs are priced in USD, creating chronic currency exposure. Argentina has 24 provinces plus the Autonomous City of Buenos Aires (CABA), each operating its own public hospital network with an independent procurement system — 25 parallel systems with no national consolidation. Fiscal capacity varies dramatically: CABA and Buenos Aires Province have dedicated high-cost drug bureaus, while smaller provinces (Formosa, Chaco, Santiago del Estero, La Rioja) report chronic chemotherapy stockouts. Approximately 36% of the population relies solely on the public sector. The Consejo Federal de Salud (COFESA) exists as a dialogue forum among provincial health ministers but has no binding policymaking authority.

(2) Public hospitals operate within the public system but manage their own pharmacy stock independently and are the primary point of dispensing for uninsured patients.

(3) Obras sociales nacionales — Argentina's national social health insurance funds, tied to union membership and formal employment. There are approximately 290–300 obras sociales nacionales, each negotiating its own drug contracts independently. Financial health varies widely: smaller union-based funds are fragile and defer purchases when the exchange rate deteriorates. The Superintendencia de Servicios de Salud (SSS) registers and supervises all obras sociales under Laws 23.660 and 23.661. SSS cancelled two additional obras sociales for irregularities in June 2025. The obras sociales subsystem represents approximately 3.5–4% of GDP within Argentina's ~9.5% total health spending. Each entity maintains its own coverage list subject to the PMO (Programa Médico Obligatorio — the mandatory minimum benefit package); standard oncology cytotoxics (cisplatin, doxorubicin, carboplatin, trastuzumab for HER2+ indications) are PMO-mandated. High-cost biologics, newer immunotherapies, and CDK4/6 inhibitors are often denied and force judicial escalation. There is no central formulary visibility across the 290–300 entities and no unified shortage monitoring system. DNU 70/2023 (see below) introduced a free-choice rule removing the prior 1-year lock-in for obra social selection, enabling immediate adverse selection by healthier/higher-income workers into private prepagas — this weakens the solidarity cross-subsidy pool that finances catastrophic oncology drugs through the FSR (Fondo Solidario de Redistribución) and the SUR/SURGE reimbursement mechanism.

The SUR/SURGE mechanism: The Sistema Único de Reintegro (SUR) was replaced by the Sistema Único de Reintegro por Gestión de Enfermedades (SURGE) under SSS Resolution 731/2023 (March 29, 2023). SURGE provides federal reimbursement to obras sociales for catastrophic high-cost drugs. The formula change from SUR to SURGE negatively affected 201 of the ~300 obras sociales. SURGE caps lag inflation and FX movements, meaning smaller obras sociales are technically insolvent for many high-cost cancer biologics in real terms.

(4) Obras sociales provinciales, such as IOMA (Instituto de Obra Médico Asistencial, Province of Buenos Aires), are large institutionally distinct actors with their own procurement logic, formularies, and budget constraints.

(5) PAMI (Programa de Atención Médica Integral / INSSJP — Instituto Nacional de Servicios Sociales para Jubilados y Pensionados) is the national social insurance program for approximately 5 million retirees and pensioners, making it one of the largest single purchasers of oncology drugs in Argentina. PAMI's formulary decisions and procurement timing have systemic effects on national drug availability. PAMI Resolution 2431/24 preserved oncology coverage at 100% (no patient copayment for oncology drugs). A January 2025 La Nación investigation found PAMI drug prices running 16x above benchmark for certain products; a separate analysis found cancer drugs in Argentina priced 123% above UK average for high-priced agents (ASCO Global Oncology 2018). As of 2025, PAMI carries an estimated AR$400 billion in outstanding debt to pharmacies — an arrears figure that directly constrains pharmacy willingness to dispense before payment is received.

(6) Banco Nacional de Drogas Especiales (BNDE) is a federal program of the Ministry of Health of the Nation that procures and distributes oncology medicines for patients treated in public hospitals who lack health coverage (no obra social, no PAMI, no prepaga). BNDE is the federal equity backstop for the uninsured. It covers more than 100 oncology drugs across more than 100 oncological conditions, including cisplatin, doxorubicin, carboplatin, and trastuzumab (for HER2+ breast cancer under defined criteria). Medicines are free at the point of care for eligible patients. The program served approximately 5,000 patients with exclusively public coverage in fiscal year 2023 (Argentina.gob.ar). Operating procedures were updated by Resolution 3377/2022 (Boletín Oficial Dec 6, 2022). BNDE tracks patient requests via the SISA (Sistema Integrado de Información Sanitaria Argentino) system. BNDE is less subject to payer-debt dynamics than PAMI because it operates on direct annual Treasury allocation rather than a provider-claim reimbursement cycle — however, the 2024–2025 Milei austerity fiscal contraction reportedly delayed some BNDE deliveries, and the Dec 2023 devaluation eroded its peso purchasing power against USD-priced biologics. Crucially, BNDE serves only patients without any other formal coverage: insured patients (even if their insurer denies the drug) cannot access BNDE and must instead pursue amparo.

(7) Empresas de medicina prepaga — private health insurance companies (Swiss Medical, Galeno, OSDE-Binario, etc.). Prepagas serve a higher-income segment and have somewhat more budget flexibility than smaller obras sociales. DNU 70/2023 (December 2023) eliminated regulatory authority over prepaga fee increases, removing prior SSS/Decree 743/2022 price caps. This produced large prepaga premium increases in 2024, forcing many enrolled patients to downgrade or switch back to obras sociales, with significant cross-system effects.

(8) Private out-of-pocket purchases through pharmacies, representing the smallest share of oncology consumption but the dominant channel in practice for patients who fall between all other options and can afford USD-priced drugs.

JUDICIAL ACCESS: AMPARO DE SALUD

A ninth de facto mechanism — the amparo de salud — functions as the primary access route for patients who are insured but whose insurer (obra social, PAMI, or prepaga) denies, delays, or is institutionally unable to provide a drug. Amparo de salud is a fast-track constitutional remedy under Article 43 of the Argentine Constitution and Law 16.986. Patients (typically represented by an attorney) file in federal or provincial court; the judge issues a medida cautelar (interim injunction) ordering the payer to provide the drug pending trial. Mean time from filing to drug delivery is approximately 150 days (Alcaraz et al. 2024, Medicina Buenos Aires 84(3):445-458). In a 405-case sample from federal/provincial high-cost-drug databases covering 2017–2020, the top litigated drugs were nusinersen (21.7% of cases, SMA, ~$750k first year), palbociclib (5.9%, breast cancer CDK4/6 inhibitor), and agalsidase-alfa (4.7%, Fabry disease). Notably, 30.6% of drugs ordered by courts were not yet ANMAT-approved at the time of the ruling. Public spending on high-cost drugs grew from approximately AR$204 million (2018) to AR$4.5 billion (2022), reflecting in part court-mandated provisions. Amparo-mandated obligations are senior to ordinary procurement and contribute to payer debt crises.

Critically for OncoSupply: Alcaraz et al. found that national graphic media appearances (Clarín, La Nación, Infobae, Página 12, Ámbito Financiero) temporally preceded amparo filings by approximately 1–3 months for nusinersen and palbociclib (Figure 1, p.456). The paper's conclusion states explicitly: "Los medios de comunicación anticiparon los procesos judiciales" (mass media anticipated the judicial processes). This is peer-reviewed independent empirical support for the OncoSupply thesis that media monitoring provides early warning of drug access failures. The mechanism: media coverage of a new expensive treatment creates patient demand, which precedes both market approval events and subsequent legal escalation when payers deny coverage. A companion paper by the same group (Boni S, Marin GH et al. "Characterization of the drug supply in Argentina." Medicina (B Aires) 2023;83:522-32) addresses supply — as opposed to access denial — and is likely more directly relevant to shortage prediction.

In September 2023, Decree 468/2023 created CATPROS (Consejo de Asistencia Técnica para Procesos Judiciales de Salud), an advisory body within the Ministry of Health that provides non-binding scientific evidence reports to courts in amparo cases involving rare diseases, orphan drugs, and innovative therapies under Law 26.689. Resolution 4168/2023 (Boletín Oficial Dec 8, 2023) implemented CATPROS. The intent is to reduce amparo overflow by giving courts technical evidence; patient organizations (notably FADEPOF) have raised concerns that CATPROS could delay access rather than improve it.

DNU 70/2023 AND REGULATORY DEREGULATION

President Javier Milei issued Decree of Necessity and Urgency (DNU) 70/2023 on December 21, 2023 (published December 29, 2023). This is the most structurally significant health regulatory change in recent Argentine history. Key provisions affecting oncology drug access:

- Obras sociales (Law 23.660 amendment): full legal, financial, and administrative autonomy confirmed; free-choice rule removes 1-year lock-in, enabling immediate competitive adverse selection.
- Generic prescription mandate (Law 25.649 amendment): all medical prescriptions must use the International Nonproprietary Name (DCI/INN), supporting biosimilar/generic substitution including biosimilar trastuzumab.
- Prepagas (Law 26.682 amendment): removal of SSS regulatory authority over fee increases.
- Drug price deregulation: removes laboratory price controls; pharmaceutical sector operates under free pricing for laboratories.

The Senate rejected DNU 70/2023 on March 14, 2024 (vote 42–25), but the Chamber of Deputies did not vote to reject within the constitutional window, so the DNU remained in force. The Ley de Bases (Law 27.742, June 2024) subsequently codified portions of DNU 70/2023. As of 2025, DNU 70/2023 is largely in force with judicial review ongoing on individual provisions — multiple injunctions have been filed by unions and consumer groups challenging specific articles. Judicial status at article level is uncertain.

ANMAT REGISTRATION AND 2025 REFORMS

ANMAT (Administración Nacional de Medicamentos, Alimentos y Tecnología Médica) is Argentina's drug regulatory authority. No drug may be commercially distributed without a valid ANMAT registration. Standard timelines: approximately 12–18 months for generics; approximately 24 months for biologics. ANMAT Provision 3752/2025 (part of the 2025 Milei deregulation package) eliminated over 300 administrative procedures and streamlined registration processes. Disposition 1741/2025 (March 2025) introduced a modernized biosimilar comparability framework, clarifying requirements for biosimilar trastuzumab and other monoclonal antibodies. These 2025 reforms are consistent with the DNU 70/2023 deregulation philosophy but were issued under separate ANMAT regulatory authority. The first trastuzumab biosimilar approved in Argentina reached market prior to 2020; by 2021, multiple biosimilar trastuzumab brands were marketed.

ANMAT does maintain exceptional importation and compassionate access mechanisms for individual patients (including authorization of non-registered products), but these do not function as operational replacements for population-level supply and cannot address systemic shortages at scale.

CURRENCY RISK AND IMPORT CHRONOLOGY

Currency risk operates as a constant background pressure on all import-dependent channels. Oncology drugs are traded in USD globally. Argentina's procurement budgets — public hospitals, obras sociales, and PAMI — are denominated in Argentine pesos. The relevant chronology:

- 2016–Aug 2019: liberalized FX under Macri/Sturzenegger.
- Sept 2019: CEPO reimposed (BCRA Communication A 6770).
- 2020–2023: CEPO 2.0 progressively tightened; multiple parallel exchange rates (oficial, blue, MEP, CCL, solidario); 30–35% PAIS tax on FX purchases.
- October 2023: SIRA (Decreto/Resolution AFIP 5466/23) introduced multi-agency import licensing, severely blocking pharmaceutical imports.
- December 27, 2023: SEDI replaced SIRA under Milei's DNU 70/2023 framework — automatic statistical declaration only; pharmaceutical imports given priority 30-day FX payment schedule.
- February 26, 2025: SEDI eliminated via ARCA RG 5651/2025.
- April 14, 2025: Main cepo cambiario lifted (BCRA Communication A 8226); individuals can purchase unlimited USD; most corporate FX operations freed.

The December 2023 devaluation (approximately 54% peso depreciation) simultaneously reduced purchasing power across PAMI, obras sociales, and BNDE for all USD-priced biologics. Combined with 2024 annual inflation of 178% YoY, this produced the largest single-quarter pharmaceutical price shock in recent Argentine history. Cancer drug prices in pesos increased approximately 50%+ in the December 2023–February 2024 window as laboratories caught up to import-cost-aligned prices after the removal of price controls.

The cisplatin/carboplatin global shortage of 2023–2024 (driven by Indian API manufacturing issues and US FDA-forced plant shutdowns) was superimposed on this domestic structural environment, creating compounded scarcity for two of the three most-used solid-tumor chemotherapy agents.

INFORMATION VISIBILITY AND STRUCTURAL ACCESS GAP

No single entity in Argentina has complete, integrated, real-time operational visibility into national oncology drug stock across all channels. Regulatory traceability systems exist and allow reconstruction of unit movements, but traceability is not equivalent to a national inventory dashboard. No public body can currently determine in operational time which hospital or payer holds transferable available stock to resolve a specific therapeutic emergency.

A critical distinction in the Argentine system is between formal coverage and real access. Many oncology treatments are nominally covered by obras sociales, PAMI, or prepagas. However, actual patient access depends on a downstream chain: clinical authorization, processing of the reimbursement file (expediente), contract with the supplier, dispensing logistics, and effective physical delivery. Each step introduces latency and risk of failure independent of whether a drug is "available" in aggregate. The gap between formal coverage and effective access is a structural feature of the Argentine health system, not an exceptional failure mode. Supply chain models that treat formal coverage as equivalent to access will systematically underestimate shortage risk.

REGIONAL ACCESS DELAY CONTEXT (FIFARMA WAIT 2024)

The Argentine access gap must be understood in the regional context quantified by the FIFARMA/IQVIA Patient W.A.I.T. Indicator 2024 (third edition, October 2024), which analyzed 365 molecules representing 80% of new active substances launched globally between 2014 and 2023 across 10 Latin American countries including Argentina (ANMAT measured). Mean time from FDA/EMA approval to public availability across Latin America: 4.75 years. For Argentina specifically, only 35% of internationally approved medicines have full or partial public availability through the formal health system. For medicines approved internationally as of 2020 — the most recent cohort — less than 12% have full or partial public availability in any of the 10 countries studied. Most critically for oncology and rare diseases: 87% of treatments for orphan and oncological diseases had no progress in either regulatory approval or public access in the 2023–2024 measurement year — the worst-performing therapeutic area in the entire study.

This means that for the standard chemotherapy drugs modeled in this project (cisplatin, doxorubicin, carboplatin), the access problem is primarily one of payer affordability and channel fragmentation — these drugs are old enough to be in formularies. However, for innovative oncology drugs (CDK4/6 inhibitors, immune checkpoint inhibitors, HER2-targeted agents beyond trastuzumab IV), Argentina's 4.75-year mean access delay means the amparo de salud mechanism is the primary real-world access route, not the formal procurement system. The 87% no-progress rate for orphan/oncology drugs directly explains why Argentina's amparo filings concentrate on newer biologics (palbociclib 5.9% of cases, nusinersen 21.7%) rather than on cisplatin or carboplatin.

When a public hospital runs out of a critical oncology drug, patients without insurance cannot access obras sociales or prepaga channels. The typical sequence involves attempting to source within the same hospital network; consulting BNDE or provincial drug banks; modifying the treatment protocol if clinically acceptable; delaying the treatment cycle; or — in the most severe cases — filing an amparo de salud. Emergency mechanisms include direct purchases outside tender cycles, inter-service drug loans within hospital networks, and informal logistics coordinated by clinicians, pharmacists, social workers, and patients' families. These semi-formal mechanisms resolve many individual cases but are not scalable, not systematically tracked, and not a substitute for institutional supply chain reliability.

HTA ARCHITECTURE: CONETEC AND THE MARIN COST-OPPORTUNITY ARGUMENT

SOURCE: Marin GH. "High-cost drugs in health systems: discussion of individual rights and collective rights." Revista Panamericana de Salud Pública (RPSP). 2024;48:e32. DOI 10.26633/RPSP.2024.32. Author affiliation: CUFAR (Centro Universitario de Farmacología), UNLP; WHO/PAHO collaborating center. Marin evaluates drugs for Argentina's CONETEC.

Argentina established CONETEC (Comisión Nacional de Evaluación de Tecnologías Sanitarias y Excelencia Clínica) under Decree 344/2023. CONETEC is the national HTA body tasked with systematic cost-effectiveness assessment of new health technologies, replacing ad-hoc Ministry-level coverage decisions. CUFAR (Marin's research center at UNLP) provides technical drug evaluation support to CONETEC. Equivalent HTA bodies exist across LATAM: IETS in Colombia, ETES in Ecuador, IETSI in Peru.

THE COST-OPPORTUNITY ARGUMENT: Marin's central thesis is that individual amparo-mandated coverage of high-cost oncology drugs imposes a structural negative externality on collective health budgets. The mechanism: amparo rulings are senior obligations that pull from the same finite public health budget. Marin calculates that covering Argentina with nivolumab+ipilimumab (immune checkpoint combination) at international prices would cost approximately US$189 million per year — equivalent to 3x the combined national TB and HIV program budget. A CDK4/6 inhibitor-based regimen for metastatic breast cancer (MMAP protocol in Colombia, palbociclib + letrozole vs. standard letrozole) generates a marginal QALY gain of 0.73 at an additional cost of US$543 million per year for the affected population. Marin cites these figures to argue that courts granting individual rights in isolation — without systematic HTA evaluation — make allocation decisions with system-level consequences that no individual judge can assess.

COURT BEHAVIOR AND HTA OVERRIDE: Courts grant drug access in approximately 94.7% of amparo/SUS cases (Kretzschmar 2024 Brazil; Argentina rates similar per Alcaraz 2024 case analysis). Crucially, courts routinely grant access to drugs that have not passed their country's own HTA criteria. In Brazil, 94.7% of granted Zolgensma lawsuits would not meet Conitec's own clinical protocol. In Argentina, 30.6% of court-ordered drugs were not yet ANMAT-approved at the time of the ruling (Alcaraz et al. 2024). Judges lack pharmacoeconomic expertise and apply a rights-based framework that treats access to any potentially beneficial treatment as constitutionally mandated regardless of cost-effectiveness.

PROPOSED SYSTEMIC SOLUTIONS (cited in Marin 2024): (1) HTA-based rational drug selection — CONETEC evaluations formally incorporated into PMO updates and amparo jurisprudence; (2) Risk-sharing agreements between pharmaceutical manufacturers and payers — outcome-based payments or price-volume arrangements to make high-cost drugs financially sustainable; (3) Centralized purchasing consortia — Uruguay's Fondo Nacional de Recursos (FNR) cited as the regional benchmark, functioning as a single-payer for high-cost technologies with lower judicialization rates than Argentina, Colombia, or Brazil; (4) Transnational price negotiation across LATAM countries — proposed but not implemented.

ONCO-SUPPLY IMPLICATION: The Marin cost-opportunity framework explains why Argentina's payer insolvency is structural, not cyclical. Obras sociales and PAMI are not simply underfunded in a temporary sense — they face a categorical cost asymmetry between the oncology drugs now standard-of-care internationally (immune checkpoint inhibitors, CDK4/6 inhibitors, HER2-targeted agents) and the premium-equivalent budgets those drugs require. The SURGE reimbursement mechanism (SSS Resolution 731/2023) that negatively affected 201 of ~300 obras sociales reflects this mismatch: SURGE caps were calibrated to historical drug costs before the immunotherapy generation made the standard of care an order of magnitude more expensive. OncoSupply's channel fragmentation analysis must treat payer insolvency for innovative oncology drugs as a baseline structural condition, not a shock. For standard cytotoxics (cisplatin, carboplatin, doxorubicin), payer insolvency is less the primary risk; manufacturing concentration and currency exposure are. For innovative agents, the access failure mode is payer refusal → amparo → 150-day delay, and OncoSupply's news monitoring pipeline (media → amparo lead-time finding in Alcaraz 2024) is the relevant early warning tool.
