The (Q,r) inventory model used in this project's Monte Carlo simulation is grounded in stochastic operations research methods for pharmaceutical supply chain disruption modeling. Key methodological references and explicit model limitations are documented here for use in brief generation, confidence statements, and honest disclosure to clients.

CORE SIMULATION REFERENCES

Badejo & Ierapetritou (2022) published "Mathematical Programming Approach to Optimize Tactical and Operational Supply Chain Decisions under Disruptions" in Industrial & Engineering Chemistry Research (Vol. 61, No. 45, pp. 16747–16763). This paper establishes that disruptions materially alter optimal (Q,r) reorder policy parameters and that tactical buffer strategies must account for both disruption probability and duration. It provides the theoretical basis for using stochastic lead-time and disruption-duration distributions — rather than deterministic inputs — in oncology supply risk modeling. Critically, the geometric distribution for disruption duration is adopted from this framework: given a disruption is ongoing, the probability of resolution at each time step is constant (memoryless property), which is appropriate for policy-driven shocks (export restrictions, currency controls) where recovery timing is not duration-dependent.

Badejo & Ierapetritou (2023) extended this in "A Mathematical Modeling Approach for Supply Chain Management under Disruption and Operational Uncertainty" (AIChE Journal, doi:10.1002/aic.18037). Their framework demonstrates that pharmaceutical supply chains face compounding uncertainty from demand variability, lead-time variability, and episodic disruption simultaneously — the same structure modeled in this project.

Badejo & Ierapetritou (2025) applied CVaR (Conditional Value at Risk) and related coherent risk measures to pharmaceutical supply chain optimization ("Optimization of Pharmaceutical Supply Chains: Navigating Disruptions and Operational Uncertainty Utilizing Risk Measures," AIChE Journal 2025;71(6):e18770, doi:10.1002/aic.18770). CVaR_α = E[stockout days | stockout days ≥ VaR_α]. This project reports CVaR_90: the expected stockout days in the worst 10% of simulation runs. CVaR_90 is a superior risk communication metric to mean stockout days because it captures tail severity — a drug with mean=7d but CVaR_90=45d has a fundamentally different risk profile than one with mean=7d and CVaR_90=12d, and the difference drives procurement buffer sizing decisions.

Izen ML, McKoy JM, Martin L, et al. (2025) "Mitigating the Impact of Drug Shortages in Oncology: Lessons Learned From the 2023 Shortages of Carboplatin and Cisplatin." Cancer Journal 31(5):e0786. doi:10.1097/PPO.0000000000000786. PMID: 41002874. This paper documents the 2023 U.S. shortage driven by Intas Pharmaceuticals' plant shutdown, which eliminated approximately 50% of U.S. cisplatin supply and simultaneously triggered carboplatin shortage through substitution demand. Key parameterization derived from this source: fill_rate=0.55 for the API export restriction scenario (hospitals received 40–60% of orders at shortage peak); lead_time_multiplier=2.5 (lead times approximately doubled to tripled during the shortage window). The shortage lasted approximately February 2023–June 2024 for cisplatin, establishing the disruption_duration_mean=90 days (geometric distribution mean) used for this scenario.

Malta M, Christian M, Cadwallader AB (2025) "Oncology Drug Shortages: Impacts, Policy Reforms, and Advocacy Imperatives." Cancer Journal, PMC12459138. One Indian manufacturing facility (Intas Pharmaceuticals, Ahmedabad) produced 24% of U.S. cisplatin supply and 8% of carboplatin supply as of January 2025 — establishing the correlated disruption mechanism. The simulate_correlated_pair() function in this project applies a shared disruption window to both drugs per simulation run, producing empirical correlation r≈0.87 between the two drugs' stockout distributions under API export restriction.

Resilient inventory management under demand disruption (Computers and Industrial Engineering 2023) identifies service level and stockout frequency as the primary operational KPIs for pharmaceutical supply risk quantification, validating this project's use of stockout days/year and unit service level as primary outputs.

MULTI-ECHELON LIMITATION AND FUTURE METHODOLOGY

Clark, AJ and Scarf, H (1960) "Optimal Policies for a Multi-Echelon Inventory Problem." Management Science 6(4):475–490. doi:10.1287/mnsc.6.4.475. This foundational paper proves that echelon-based inventory policies (where each node in a supply chain optimizes based on total downstream inventory, not just its own stock) are optimal for serial supply chains. The current project uses a single-echelon (Q,r) model — a deliberate simplification that treats the entire supply chain as one inventory node at the hospital/EPS level.

The single-echelon approximation is appropriate for generating shortage risk signals at the point of care but has a known bias: it cannot distinguish between a distributor-level shortage (Audifarma, Cruz Verde, Cafam holding no stock) and a hospital-level shortage (EPS has stock but hasn't authorized dispensing). In the Colombian EPS debt cascade context, the physical drug may exist in a distributor warehouse but be withheld due to unpaid invoices — a financial blockade, not a physical shortage. The single-echelon model correctly captures the patient-level outcome (no drug) but cannot identify which layer of the supply chain is the proximate failure point. For a consulting deliverable that assigns intervention responsibility, this distinction matters.

Graves, SC and Willems, SP (2000) "Optimizing Strategic Safety Stock Placement in Supply Chains." Manufacturing & Service Operations Management 2(1):68–83. doi:10.1287/msom.2.1.68.23267. This paper provides the algorithmic foundation for multi-echelon safety stock placement: given a supply network modeled as a spanning tree, the optimal safety stock allocation across all nodes can be computed by dynamic programming. Their industrial applications achieved 25–50% reductions in safety stock holding cost. The current project's single-echelon safety stock formula (SS = z × sqrt(d² × σ_L² + L × σ_D²), Warren OM Textbook §6.6) is equivalent to Graves & Willems for a one-stage network. Extending to two stages (distributor + hospital) would require modeling the service time guarantee from distributor to hospital and applying the echelon stock decomposition — a methodological enhancement for future versions.

LOST-SALES VS. BACKORDER ASSUMPTION

Zipkin, PH (2000) Foundations of Inventory Management. McGraw-Hill. The current simulation uses a lost-sales model: when daily demand exceeds available inventory, the excess demand is lost (stockout_days += 1, inventory = 0). For clinical oncology drugs, this is a deliberate conservative assumption — it treats every stockout day as a treatment failure, not a treatment delay. In practice, most cancer centers operate with a partial backorder system: cisplatin shortage → protocol substitution to carboplatin if available; carboplatin shortage → treatment delay by one cycle (21–28 days) if clinically acceptable; trastuzumab shortage → dose delay with documented clinical risk. The lost-sales model overstates the frequency of complete treatment failures relative to a pure backorder model, and understates total cost relative to a mixed model where delays accumulate as a hidden liability. This overstates risk slightly but is the safer assumption for a consulting risk brief — clients prefer conservative warnings over false reassurance.

DEMAND DISTRIBUTION LIMITATION

The simulation uses normally distributed daily demand (truncated at 0). For high-volume generic drugs (cisplatin, doxorubicin, carboplatin) at a mid-size cancer center, the central limit theorem justifies a normal approximation across daily aggregate demand from multiple patients. For trastuzumab — 1.5 doses/day mean — the normal approximation is weaker: at this volume, demand is more accurately characterized as intermittent (Poisson or negative binomial), with discrete patient-cycle timing. The current model's trastuzumab CV=0.47 partially compensates by inflating variance, but does not capture the lumpy pattern that arises from HER2+ patients on 3-week treatment cycles. Future model improvement: replace normal distribution with a compound Poisson demand process for biologics.

Badejo & Ierapetritou (2025) further developed "Optimization of Pharmaceutical Supply Chains: Navigating Disruptions and Operational Uncertainty Utilizing Risk Measures" (AIChE Journal, 2025;71(6):e18770, doi:10.1002/aic.18770). This paper applies CVaR (Conditional Value at Risk) and related coherent risk measures to pharmaceutical supply chain optimization under disruption scenarios. CVaR at the α confidence level is defined as the expected loss conditional on the loss exceeding the α-th percentile (Value at Risk): CVaR_α = E[X | X ≥ VaR_α]. Applied to supply simulation: CVaR_90 = expected stockout days in the worst 10% of simulation runs. CVaR is a superior risk metric to mean stockout days because it is sensitive to tail risk — a scenario with mean=7 days but CVaR_90=48 days has a very different risk profile than one with mean=7 days and CVaR_90=12 days. This project implements CVaR_90 in all simulate() outputs.

The geometric distribution for disruption duration modeling captures the "memoryless" property of disruption events: given that a disruption is ongoing, the probability of resolution at any time step is constant. This is appropriate for disruptions driven by policy shocks (export restrictions, currency controls) where recovery timing is uncertain and not duration-dependent. The geometric distribution produces heavier-tailed duration outcomes than exponential models, which is a better fit for regulatory and political disruptions that can persist for months.

CORRELATED DISRUPTION: CISPLATIN AND CARBOPLATIN SHARED API SOURCE

The most underappreciated structural risk in platinum-based chemotherapy supply chains is the correlated disruption between cisplatin and carboplatin. Both drugs share a common API manufacturing base in India. Malta et al. (Cancer Journal, 2025, PMC12459138) identified that as of January 2025, one Indian manufacturing facility (Intas Pharmaceuticals, Ahmedabad) accounted for 24% of global cisplatin supply and 8% of carboplatin supply. In December 2022–February 2023, an FDA inspection-prompted shutdown of this facility triggered the largest cisplatin shortage in recent U.S. history; the resulting substitution demand caused carboplatin to fall into simultaneous shortage (93% of U.S. academic cancer centers reported carboplatin shortage by May 2023). This is a correlated disruption: a single upstream node failure causes BOTH drugs to become unavailable simultaneously.

Independent simulation — running cisplatin and carboplatin as separate Monte Carlo experiments — underestimates joint portfolio risk because it treats the two disruptions as statistically independent events. Correct methodology (Badejo & Ierapetritou AIChE 2025) requires drawing a single shared disruption window per simulation run and applying it to both drugs. This project implements this through simulate_correlated_pair(), which forces a single geometric disruption window shared by both cisplatin and carboplatin in each run. Empirically, this produces a disruption correlation of r≈0.87 between the two drugs' stockout distributions under API export restriction scenarios — versus r≈0 under independent simulation. The joint probability of both drugs exceeding 60 critical stockout days simultaneously (joint_critical_prob) is a key portfolio risk metric not captured by independent drug analysis.

For Latin American oncology supply chains, the correlated platinum disruption is particularly severe because cisplatin and carboplatin together account for the majority of solid-tumor chemotherapy volume (breast, lung, ovarian, testicular cancers). A shared API shock that knocks out both drugs simultaneously eliminates substitution flexibility entirely, forcing protocol modifications or treatment delays. This is the primary argument for maintaining non-correlated alternative drug buffer stock (e.g., doxorubicin) at higher safety stock levels than platinum drugs.

For all Monte Carlo results in this project: 500 simulation runs per scenario are used. Stochastic lead times follow log-normal distributions parameterized by drug and country. Disruption onset follows a uniform random variable (days 1–45); disruption duration follows geometric distribution with scenario-specific mean (Badejo & Ierapetritou 2022). The (Q,r) reorder policy is optimized per drug-country-scenario triple using Warren OM Textbook §6.6 safety stock formula. CVaR_90 is reported alongside mean stockout days and P(critical) for all simulation outputs.
