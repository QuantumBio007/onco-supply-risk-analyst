Venezuela's oncology drug procurement system has experienced a structural collapse over the 2013–2024 period driven by fiscal crisis, currency controls, and institutional degradation. Unlike Argentina (fragmented but functioning) or Colombia (strained but operational), Venezuela's public procurement system is not a functioning institutional channel for oncology drugs — it is a residual structure operating far below any meaningful coverage threshold.

INSTITUTIONAL STRUCTURE

The two institutional actors are the MPPS (Ministerio del Poder Popular para la Salud), which nominally directs national drug policy and procurement, and the INHRR (Instituto Nacional de Higiene "Rafael Rangel"), which serves as the drug regulatory authority — the functional equivalent of Argentina's ANMAT or Colombia's INVIMA. INHRR was founded October 17, 1938, and is designated as a WHO National Reference Center for Registration and Control of Medicines (since 1996). All drugs sold or procured in Venezuela require a valid INHRR registration. Registrations are country-specific: ANMAT or INVIMA approval does not transfer to Venezuela.

INHRR registration timelines are officially targeted at 6–12 months for generic drugs but in practice take 18–36 months or longer due to backlog, staff attrition, and resource constraints. Biologics historically required 24–48 months. In March 2023, INHRR launched SIVERC (Sistema Venezolano de Evaluación y Registro de Productos Sanitarios), an online electronic registration platform intended to modernize and accelerate review. The INHRR Review Board (Junta Revisora) maintains a critical exception mechanism: special authorization for importation of non-registered pharmaceutical products, valid for a maximum of 6 months per authorization. This is the primary legal route for innovative oncology and rare-disease drugs that have not completed full Venezuelan sanitary registration. Once INHRR grants authorization, MPPS issues the import permit; licensed importers or NGOs then execute the import.

The IVSS (Instituto Venezolano de los Seguros Sociales) operates pensioner pharmacies (Farmacias de Alto Costo / Farmacias IVSS) for insured workers, but supply to these pharmacies has largely collapsed. SUMED (state pharmaceutical distributor) is largely defunct. Private distributors include Drolanca, Droguería Los Andes, and pharmacy chains Farmatodo, Locatel, and Farmahorro — these function in the dollarized private sector.

BUDGET COLLAPSE

Budget collapse is the defining feature of the system. Healthcare spending fell from an estimated 8.1–9.1% of total government expenditure in 2006–2010 to approximately 5.8% by 2014, and public health spending reached approximately 1.18% of GDP by 2017 (Ballard Brief, BYU, 2023; The Lancet). The Venezuelan government allocated approximately $200 million annually for pharmaceutical imports in 2014 against an estimated minimum requirement of $1 billion (Ballard Brief, 2023). Medicine imports fell by approximately 70% between 2012 and 2016. The pharmaceutical sector was operating at only 38.4% of installed capacity in Q3 2023 (Conindustria/Cifar). As of September 2025, El Nacional reported "massive debt with suppliers and closure of international credit lines" resulting from chronic foreign-currency shortage for sanitary imports.

ONCOLOGY DRUG AVAILABILITY

For oncology drugs specifically, the Lancet Oncology (2017) reported that Venezuela had available only approximately 10% of the medicines needed for cancer care. This figure is independently confirmed by Duma & Duque Duran (Journal of Global Oncology 2019, PMC6550090), who document cisplatin, bevacizumab, aromatase inhibitors, and tamoxifen as specifically unavailable or subject to extreme intermittency in Venezuelan public hospitals. Doxorubicin and carboplatin are chronically short. Trastuzumab is unavailable in the public sector for most patients throughout 2020–2025 and is accessible only via private dollarized purchase or NGO emergency supply. Approximately 4,000 pediatric oncology patients per year contact NGO networks for medication, with fewer than 10% obtaining oncology drugs through formal channels (Global Cancer Institute reporting).

Updated shortage data: Convite's March 2024 monthly monitoring bulletin reported 28.4% overall medicine shortage (up from 26.3% reported in August 2023 cited by Human Rights Watch 2024). The Encuesta Nacional de Hospitales (ENH, September 2024), conducted by Médicos por la Salud, found 37.4% medicine shortage in public health centers — a higher figure reflecting institutional-level measurement rather than patient-reported access. Hospital-level shortages historically reached 85–95% in 2018–2019. The partial recovery to 28–37% shortage rates since 2022–2024 reflects the normalization of a dollarized private economy rather than a restoration of public procurement capacity.

Compounding drug unavailability: the 2019 article documents that hundreds of oncologists have emigrated (brain drain), leaving major oncology programs understaffed; a "black market recycle system" for opioids (donated by families of deceased cancer patients) has emerged as a de facto palliative care mechanism; 87% of the population lives at or below the poverty line; and 79% of hospitals had interruptions in water service (Central University of Venezuela 2018), undermining cold-chain integrity for biologics. As of August 2023, 72% of the Venezuelan population reported being unable to access public health services when needed (Convite, cited by HRW 2024).

CURRENCY AND PAYMENT ENVIRONMENT

A critical structural feature is that import approvals in Venezuela are tied to foreign-exchange allocations controlled by the national government. Venezuela has undergone de facto dollarization since approximately 2019: the BCV (Banco Central de Venezuela) maintains an official rate, but private market transactions use the parallel/monitor rate. The successive currency control systems (CADIVI, CENCOEX, DICOM) have historically restricted hard-currency access for pharmaceutical importers. Minimum wage and pension stood at 130 bolivars since March 2022, equivalent to approximately $3.60 per month at the official rate (Cifar). These are permanent structural barriers, not scenario-specific shocks.

The pharmaceutical market grew 7.6% in 2023 and 23.4% YoY in H1 2025 (190.22 million units), entirely driven by dollarized private-sector demand — not a recovery of public procurement capacity. Out-of-pocket expenditure reached an estimated 62.99% of total health spending in 2017 (Ballard Brief), compared to a Latin American regional average of approximately 28%.

DIASPORA AND INFORMAL PROCUREMENT

In practice, oncology drugs in Venezuela are accessed primarily through: (1) private, dollarized out-of-pocket purchase — affordable only for a small fraction of the population; (2) international NGO and donation programs; (3) diaspora-driven informal procurement; and (4) residual MPPS/IVSS formal channels where they function at all.

The diaspora mechanism has become quantitatively significant. The Venezuelan diaspora reached approximately 7.7 million people abroad (R4V/UNHCR 2024). Total remittances reached approximately $3–5.4 billion in 2023 (Ecoanalitica/Inter-American Dialogue), equivalent to approximately 3–6% of GDP. Of remittance-receiving households, 58% use remittances for healthcare (Encovi 2023, UCAB), and 94% for food. The Encovi survey implies diaspora health financing may approach or exceed public health spending as a share of GDP.

Three principal informal drug procurement channels operate through diaspora networks: (1) Zelle-mediated USD transfers via "casas de Zelle" — Venezuelan retail establishments with US bank accounts where recipients collect physical USD minus commission, then used to buy drugs at private pharmacies (Farmatodo, Locatel, Farmahorro, SAAS); (2) cryptocurrency remittances — $461 million+ in 2023 (Bloomberg), using stablecoins (USDT, USDC) and Bitcoin via Binance, Reserve, and Airtm; (3) direct in-kind digital remittances — relatives abroad order drugs from Venezuelan pharmacy chain online portals (Farmatodo.com) for local pickup or delivery; and (4) physical mule/courier shipments from the US, Spain, Colombia, and cross-border purchase trips to Cúcuta (Colombia) or Boa Vista (Brazil). The diaspora channel bypasses formulary restrictions entirely and sources any drug available in private pharmacies regardless of public formulary status. However, it benefits only households with diaspora connections — approximately 30–45% of the population by various estimates — and is unaffordable for the remaining majority.

NGO PIPELINES

Major NGO pipelines supplement access for targeted populations: Convite AC publishes monthly Right-to-Health monitoring bulletins (since 2018) and coordinates limited drug distribution; Acción Solidaria receives approximately 1,500 monthly medication requests; Codevida advocates for and sources emergency supply for HIV, transplant, and cancer patients; MSF has operated border facilities since 2019 (50,000+ consultations); Cáritas Venezuela distributes nutrition and medical aid. These NGOs collectively cover a fraction of the total unmet need for oncology drugs.

US OFAC SANCTIONS AND HUMANITARIAN CARVE-OUTS

OFAC Venezuela sanctions (31 CFR Part 591, since Executive Order 13808, August 2017) bar most US-person dealings with the Government of Venezuela. Three humanitarian general licenses were issued August 5, 2019, providing permanent authorizations with no expiration date:

- General License 4C: authorizes exports of all agricultural commodities, medicine, and medical devices from the US (including via third-country resellers) to Venezuela.
- General License 26: authorizes emergency medical services involving Venezuelan government counterparties (e.g., MPPS hospital staff dealing with US-supplied equipment).
- General License 29: authorizes NGO humanitarian projects (Convite, Codevida, MSF-USA, Catholic Relief Services, Direct Relief, Project HOPE can operate without specific OFAC license).

These humanitarian carve-outs are legally robust and have remained intact through all subsequent sanctions adjustments (including GL 44/GL 44A oil-sector changes in 2023–2024, and GL 41A/5R/41B in July 2025). The practical limitation is banking de-risking: many US banks decline Venezuela-related transactions even when legally authorized under GL 4C, raising pharmaceutical import compliance overhead by an estimated 5–15% above benchmark and causing payment routing delays. Some manufacturers have exited the Venezuelan market not because of legal prohibition but due to compliance and payment friction. The UN Special Rapporteur on unilateral coercive measures (Alena Douhan, 2021 report A/HRC/48/59) concluded that US sanctions contributed to humanitarian access constraints despite the carve-outs; HRW and others maintain the domestic budget collapse is the primary cause predating sanctions.

RISK CHARACTERIZATION AND SIMULATION IMPLICATIONS

Venezuela does not represent a high-risk variant of a functioning Latin American procurement system. It represents a system where the baseline is effectively a sustained crisis, and additional scenario shocks (API export restriction, currency devaluation) produce marginal rather than dramatic increases in stockout risk — because the baseline is already near the floor. The parameters used in this project's Monte Carlo model — 30% effective budget cap, 60% structural fill rate, 10-day initial stock — reflect this baseline collapse, not a scenario shock layered on top of a functioning system.

The implication for supply chain risk modeling is that structural parameters dominate scenario parameters in Venezuela. This is reflected in the simulation results: Venezuela Baseline = 31 stockout days/year (HIGH), close in absolute terms to Venezuela Combined Shock = 35 stockout days/year. Incremental disruption scenarios add little to an already-collapsing system.
