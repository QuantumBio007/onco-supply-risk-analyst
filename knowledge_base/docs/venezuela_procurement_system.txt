Venezuela's oncology drug procurement system has experienced a structural collapse over the 2013–2024 period driven by fiscal crisis, currency controls, and institutional degradation. Unlike Argentina (fragmented but functioning) or Colombia (strained but operational), Venezuela's public procurement system is not a functioning institutional channel for oncology drugs — it is a residual structure operating far below any meaningful coverage threshold.

The two institutional actors are the MPPS (Ministerio del Poder Popular para la Salud), which nominally directs national drug policy and procurement, and the INHRR (Instituto Nacional de Higiene "Rafael Rangel"), which serves as the drug regulatory authority — the functional equivalent of Argentina's ANMAT or Colombia's INVIMA. All drugs sold or procured in Venezuela require a valid INHRR registration. Registrations are country-specific: ANMAT or INVIMA approval in Argentina or Colombia does not transfer. INHRR registration timelines have been reported at 12–18 months or longer, creating additional lead-time barriers for any new generic oncology drug entering the market (Pharmatradz, 2024).

A critical structural feature is that import approvals in Venezuela have historically been tied to foreign-exchange allocations controlled by the national government. Since oncology drugs are traded globally in USD, and Venezuela's successive currency control systems (CADIVI, CENCOEX, DICOM) have restricted hard-currency access, pharmaceutical importers have faced severe constraints independent of any disruption scenario. This is a permanent structural barrier, not a scenario-specific shock.

Budget collapse is the defining feature of the system. Healthcare spending fell from an estimated 8.1% of GDP in 2006 to approximately 1.18% in 2017 (Ballard Brief, BYU, 2023). The Venezuelan government allocated roughly $200 million annually for pharmaceutical imports in 2014, against an estimated minimum requirement of $1 billion (Ballard Brief, 2023). Medicine imports fell by approximately 70% between 2012 and 2016 (Ballard Brief, 2023). These are structural conditions that predate any of the simulation scenarios modeled in this project. The parameters used in this project's Monte Carlo model — 30% effective budget cap, 60% structural fill rate, 10-day initial stock — reflect this baseline collapse, not a scenario shock layered on top of a functioning system.

For oncology drugs specifically, the Lancet Oncology reported in 2017 that Venezuela had available only approximately 10% of the medicines needed for cancer care. As of August 2023, overall medicine shortages stood at an estimated 26.3% (Convite, cited by Human Rights Watch, 2024) — representing a partial recovery from the acute 2017–2019 crisis period, though individual therapeutic categories vary significantly. Oncology drugs are not produced domestically in Venezuela at meaningful scale; approximately 45% of the pharmaceutical market overall is import-dependent, and for biologics and specialized oncology agents (cisplatin, carboplatin, trastuzumab), import dependence is effectively 100%. Cold-chain logistics for biologics such as trastuzumab are additionally compromised by documented power instability — 66% of Venezuela's largest hospitals lack consistent running water (IHP UK, 2023), with analogous infrastructure constraints on refrigeration.

By August 2023, 72% of the Venezuelan population reported being unable to access public health services when needed (Convite, cited by HRW, 2024). Out-of-pocket health expenditure reached an estimated 62.99% of total health spending in 2017 (Ballard Brief, 2023), compared to a Latin American regional average of approximately 28%. This means that in practice, oncology drugs in Venezuela are accessed primarily through: (1) private, dollarized out-of-pocket purchase — affordable only for a small fraction of the population given median incomes; (2) international NGO and donation programs; and (3) the IVSS (Instituto Venezolano de los Seguros Sociales, the social security system) — itself severely degraded and operating below capacity.

The implication for supply chain risk modeling is significant: Venezuela does not represent a high-risk variant of a functioning Latin American procurement system. It represents a system where the baseline is effectively a sustained crisis, and additional scenario shocks (API export restriction, currency devaluation) produce marginal rather than dramatic increases in stockout risk — because the baseline is already near the floor. This is reflected in the simulation results: Venezuela Baseline = 31 stockout days/year (HIGH), which is close in absolute terms to Venezuela Combined Shock = 35 stockout days/year. The structural parameters dominate the scenario parameters.
