Colombia's oncology drug procurement system is organized around mandatory health insurance with two regimes. The contributory regime covers formally employed workers through payroll contributions; the subsidized regime covers lower-income populations through government funding. Together these regimes extend formal coverage to the large majority of the population. The primary procurement actors are the EPS (Entidades Promotoras de Salud) — health insurers who administer both regimes and are legally responsible for ensuring enrolled patients access medications on the national formulary (Plan de Beneficios en Salud, PBS). As of 2025, 28 EPS entities are authorized; principal ones include Nueva EPS, Sanitas, Compensar, SURA, Salud Total, and Famisanar.

EPS entities procure drugs through contracts with pharmaceutical distributors (gestores farmacéuticos / operadores logísticos — principally Audifarma, Cruz Verde, Cafam, Colsubsidio, Coopidrogas) and IPS (Instituciones Prestadoras de Servicios) hospital pharmacies. ADRES (Administradora de los Recursos del Sistema General de Seguridad Social en Salud) disburses the UPC (Unidad de Pago por Capitación) capitation to EPS for PBS-covered drugs and allocates the presupuestos máximos for non-PBS drugs routed through the MIPRES system.

PBS FORMULARY COVERAGE

The PBS (Plan de Beneficios en Salud) is defined annually by MINSALUD. Resolution 2718/2024 (effective January 1, 2025) maintains PBS coverage for high-cost diseases including cancer. Conventional cytotoxic oncology drugs — cisplatin, doxorubicin, carboplatin — are PBS-covered. Trastuzumab IV is PBS-covered for approved HER2+ indications (early breast cancer, metastatic breast cancer, advanced gastric cancer). Newer high-cost oncology drugs — immune checkpoint inhibitors (pembrolizumab, nivolumab), targeted therapies (T-DM1, CDK4/6 inhibitors like palbociclib), and most novel biologics — are not on PBS and must be routed through MIPRES as non-PBS prescriptions. The PBS does not cover medications without INVIMA approval or experimental treatments. A 2026 reform reportedly moved approximately 100 specific treatments/services to an exclusion list, which is projected to substantially increase tutela demand.

MIPRES AND PRESUPUESTOS MÁXIMOS

MIPRES (Mi Prescripción) is the MINSALUD web tool through which clinicians prescribe non-PBS technologies (medications, services, procedures). For high-cost oncology drugs not on PBS, the MIPRES pathway is the primary formal channel. Two financing modalities operate: (1) presupuestos máximos — prospective annual allocations to EPS by ADRES based on prior MIPRES consumption (established by Resolution 205/2020); and (2) legacy recobro — retrospective claim for items outside the prospective scheme. Approximately 5% of the total health budget routes through the non-PBS/MIPRES channel.

The presupuestos máximos system is under severe financial stress. As of July 2024, ADRES had distributed 100% of the designated maximum-budget funds for the year, requiring additional allocation by emergency resolution (ADRES Resolution 205474 for 2024; Resolution 067/2025 for 2025 resets). Ten medicines accounted for 58% of all MIPRES prescriptions in 2023 — high concentration risk on a small number of expensive biologics. In February 2025, the Constitutional Court ordered MINSALUD to pay COP 819 billion in pending presupuestos máximos transfers to EPS. When ADRES transfers are late, EPS cannot pay distributors, triggering drug withholding downstream. MIPRES prescription denials and delays are routinely escalated through tutela (see below).

INVIMA: REGISTRATION AND BACKLOG

INVIMA (Instituto Nacional de Vigilancia de Medicamentos y Alimentos) is Colombia's drug regulatory authority. All oncology drugs must hold a valid INVIMA sanitary registration before legal marketing. Registrations are country-specific: ANMAT approval in Argentina does not transfer to Colombia.

For conventional generic drugs manufactured abroad, registration takes approximately 12–18 months (U.S. Trade.gov 2024). For domestically manufactured generics, the process runs approximately 200 days. For biologics and biosimilars — the category that includes trastuzumab — Colombia operates under Decree 1782/2014 (MINSALUD), which establishes three regulatory pathways: (1) full dossier (expediente completo); (2) comparability pathway (vía de comparabilidad) — abbreviated evaluation referencing the innovator with a full comparability exercise; (3) abbreviated comparability pathway (vía abreviada) — reduced clinical requirements where the reference product has extensive history. INVIMA is authorized to request additional information during review, granting applicants 90 days to respond — a step that routinely extends effective timelines. Biosimilar applications filed in 2020 averaged approximately 28 months to approval (GABI Journal).

INVIMA faces a documented and growing backlog: as of the first major contingency intervention in 2023–2025, over 14,000 applications were pending across all product categories. Of these, approximately 5,700 submitted before November 2023 were included in a judicial evacuation plan. Resolution 2025010547 (March 19, 2025) established a 6-month contingency plan to clear the backlog; INVIMA reported resolving 23,229 of 26,049 procedures in the November 2023–September 2025 window, substantially reducing the pending volume.

BIOSIMILAR PATHWAY

The first trastuzumab biosimilar approved in Colombia is registered under INVIMA sanitary registration 2018MTB-0018684. By 2021, four trastuzumab biosimilars were approved across Latin America, with multiple brand names marketed in Colombia. Bevacizumab biosimilars (two or more) and rituximab biosimilar CT-P10 (commercialized 2018) are also approved. Colombia has approximately 3–5 monoclonal antibody biosimilars approved as of 2025. Biosimilar uptake in EPS formularies varies by contract cycle and IETS (health technology assessment institute) recommendations. INVIMA's 2025 contingency plan included prioritization of pending biologic applications.

EPS-IPS PHARMACEUTICAL DEBT CASCADE

The most significant structural threat to de-facto oncology drug access in Colombia is the EPS-IPS-distributor pharmaceutical debt cascade — a financial blockade that produces actual patient-level drug unavailability outside of INVIMA's formal shortage classification.

Total EPS-IPS debt: approximately COP 32.9 trillion in 2024 (up from COP 25 trillion in 2023), per MINSALUD (July 15, 2025). The pharmaceutical component of this debt is approximately COP 4.2 trillion. Between 2020 and 2024, COP 15.79 trillion accumulated without traceability, including COP 4.22 trillion in 2024 alone.

Financial fragility of EPS is systemic: approximately 80% of EPS lack the legally required reserves; only 6 of 29 EPS comply with all financial regulations as of 2025. Principal distressed entities include Nueva EPS (COP 9.2 trillion in commitments, April 2025), EPS Sanitas (COP 2.88 trillion). EPS at risk of liquidation in 2025 per El Tiempo: Emssanar, Coosalud, Famisanar, SOS, Savia Salud, Asmet Salud, Sanitas, Nueva EPS.

The debt cascade mechanism: ADRES pays UPC capitation to EPS; EPS is obligated to pay distributors within 30–60 days nominally, but in practice cycles extend to 180–365+ days due to EPS insolvency. Distributors (Audifarma, Cruz Verde, Cafam, etc.) restrict drug shipments to EPS in arrears, creating a de-facto shortage at point of care. The government paid 90.3% of pharmaceutical operator invoices in 2024 (COP 13.3 trillion of COP 14.7 trillion filed), yet supply failures persisted — confirming that intermediation friction, not total funding, is the root cause. Health Superintendency 2025 inspections found tons of medicines stored in private warehouses near expiry, confirming financial blockade rather than physical scarcity. People's Health Movement characterizes this as "asfixia controlada" (controlled suffocation) — debt functioning as a bargaining chip in the political struggle over health reform.

Hundreds of cancer patients were reportedly forced to suspend chemotherapy during 2024–2025 due to non-delivery from EPS and distributors (People's Health Movement, ColombiaOne, El Colombiano). Cisplatin, doxorubicin, carboplatin, and trastuzumab are not individually listed in INVIMA's formal shortage declarations during this window — confirming that the debt-driven access failures occur outside the official shortage classification system.

SHORTAGE MONITORING

INVIMA maintains a "Desabastecimientos" centralized shortage module (since May 1, 2018), with a public dashboard. As of December 2024, INVIMA reported 8 medicines in active shortage, 5 at risk, and 181 under monitoring. The December 2024 shortage list did not include cisplatin, doxorubicin, carboplatin, or trastuzumab — a mismatch with the documented patient-level chemotherapy disruptions that reflects the limitation of the formal system in capturing debt-driven de-facto shortages. PQRS (Peticiones, Quejas, Reclamos y Solicitudes) for non-delivery of medications hit a historical monthly peak of 52,655 in January 2025. Cumulative SuperSalud medication complaints exceeded 685,000.

MINSALUD is developing SISCAATS (Sistema de Información para la Centralización de Alertas de Abastecimiento de Tecnologías en Salud), a new integrated shortage monitoring system. Under the proposed SISCAATS resolution (published for comment late 2025): EPS, IPS, and gestores farmacéuticos have 2 months to adapt upon enactment; manufacturers, importers, and wholesalers have 6 months; medical device reporting integrates within 1 year. Non-compliance is sanctioned by SuperSalud, INVIMA, or the Superintendencia de Industria y Comercio depending on actor.

TUTELA: JUDICIAL ACCESS MECHANISM

The tutela — a constitutional writ of protection under Article 86 of the Colombian Constitution — has become the primary real-world access mechanism for non-PBS oncology drugs and for PBS drugs delayed or denied by EPS. Tutela can be filed with any first-instance judge, who must rule within 10 days (48 hours for urgent care). Courts routinely order EPS to deliver drugs within 48 hours once a tutela is granted. Drugs ordered via tutela are financed through the same EPS channels — UPC for PBS drugs, presupuestos máximos recobro for non-PBS.

Tutela volumes: approximately 265,000 total filed (2024), growing to approximately 312,500 (2025), a 17.9% increase. Approximately 34% of all tutelas are health-related (~106,250 in 2025 on this basis). Of Defensoría del Pueblo-accompanied health tutelas filed January–August 2025 (18,451 total), approximately 24% related to denial of medication delivery — roughly 4,400 medication-specific tutelas in that 8-month window accompanied by the Defensoría alone. In 2023, approximately 198,000 health tutelas were filed nationally; in 2024, the Defensoría received 35,000 complaints (+32% vs. 2023). Sentencia T-377/2024 of the Corte Constitucional specifically reinforced oncology drug access through the tutela framework.

The tutela system is the direct symptom of the EPS-IPS-distributor debt cascade: when financial blockade prevents medicine flow, patients escalate to tutela. Tutela-ordered obligations function as senior claims on EPS budgets, further straining EPS finances and completing a self-reinforcing insolvency cycle.

REFORM CONTEXT

The Petro government's attempts to restructure the health system have significantly amplified uncertainty. Decree 0858/2025, which sought to convert EPS into non-intermediary "Gestoras de Salud y Vida," was provisionally suspended by the Council of State on October 23, 2025. The broader congressional health reform was blocked in December 2025. EPS entities continue operating under the existing model. Law 2360 (June 14, 2024) reinforced special protections for cancer patients, modifying the Sandra Ceballos Law (Law 1384/2010). The statutory right to health (Law 1751/2015) and Article 86 tutela remain the constitutional foundation of Colombia's judicial access mechanism.

Colombia's currency (Colombian peso, COP) is more stable than the Argentine peso but subject to USD fluctuation. Approximately 70% of pharmaceuticals sold in Colombia are imported, exposing EPS to FX-driven cost growth. Total pharmaceutical spending reached approximately COP 28.9 trillion in 2024 (ACEMI estimate) without proportional access improvement, indicating that the debt blockade absorbs spending without translating to delivery.

REGIONAL ACCESS DELAY CONTEXT (FIFARMA WAIT 2024)

Colombia's access gap must be understood in the regional context quantified by the FIFARMA/IQVIA Patient W.A.I.T. Indicator 2024 (third edition, October 2024), which analyzed 365 molecules representing 80% of new active substances launched globally between 2014 and 2023 across 10 Latin American countries including Colombia (INVIMA measured). Mean time from FDA/EMA approval to public availability across Latin America: 4.75 years. Only 35% of internationally approved medicines have full or partial public availability through formal health systems in the region. For medicines approved as of 2020, less than 12% have public availability. For oncology and rare diseases specifically: 87% of treatments for orphan and oncological diseases had no progress in regulatory approval or public access in the 2023–2024 measurement year — the worst-performing therapeutic area in the entire study.

The implication for Colombia's tutela burden is direct: the 4.75-year mean access delay from FDA/EMA to Colombian PBS inclusion explains structurally why tutelas concentrate on innovative oncology drugs (immune checkpoint inhibitors, CDK4/6 inhibitors, HER2-targeted agents beyond trastuzumab IV, CAR-T) rather than on conventional chemotherapy. The 87% no-progress rate for orphan/oncology drugs is the upstream driver of the tutela escalation trend (265,000 → 312,500 total tutelas 2024–2025). For the drugs modeled in this project — cisplatin, doxorubicin, carboplatin, trastuzumab IV — these are established therapies already in PBS and not primarily affected by the FIFARMA WAIT delay. Their access failures are driven by the EPS-IPS debt cascade and payer insolvency, not regulatory lag. However, any oncology brief for Colombia must acknowledge that the debt-driven access failure for PBS drugs coexists with a 4.75-year structural access delay for innovative drugs — two independent, additive barriers operating simultaneously.

RISK CHARACTERIZATION

Relative to Argentina and Venezuela, Colombia represents a moderate-to-high-risk procurement environment for oncology drugs. The formal regulatory and insurance infrastructure is more capable than Venezuela's (functioning INVIMA, active PBS, EPS coordination) and more centralized than Argentina's (28 EPS vs. 290–300 obras sociales). However, the EPS-IPS debt cascade creates systemic de-facto access failures for the insured population that exceed what the formal shortage monitoring system captures. The tutela volume (312,500 in 2025, 34% health) is the most reliable real-time indicator of access failure in Colombia.
