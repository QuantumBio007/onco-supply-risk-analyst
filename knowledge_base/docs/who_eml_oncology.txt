The WHO Model List of Essential Medicines (EML) is published by the World Health Organization and updated every two years. It identifies the medicines considered most effective, safe, and cost-effective for priority health conditions. For governments and health systems, EML inclusion is the primary international signal that a drug should be stocked, procured, and made accessible to patients.

Oncology section history: The WHO EML historically focused on infectious diseases and primary care. In 2015, a landmark expansion added a dedicated cancer medicines section, recognizing that oncology drugs are essential, not optional luxuries. This expansion first included trastuzumab on the complementary EML list. In 2019, trastuzumab was transferred to the WHO EML core list — the definitive addition that confirmed it as a globally essential medicine regardless of cost. The 2019 core-list addition is the internationally recognized milestone for trastuzumab's EML status.

Drugs relevant to this project on the WHO EML (23rd edition, 2023):
- Cisplatin: on EML since 1979. Platinum-based chemotherapy for multiple cancer types.
- Doxorubicin: on EML. Anthracycline for breast cancer, leukemia, lymphoma, sarcoma.
- Carboplatin: on EML. Second-generation platinum compound.
- Methotrexate: on EML. Antimetabolite for leukemia, breast cancer, osteosarcoma.
- Trastuzumab: added to WHO EML core list in 2019 (first appeared on complementary list 2015). Monoclonal antibody for HER2-positive breast and gastric cancers.

What EML inclusion means in practice:
1. Procurement priority: health ministries are expected to ensure availability of EML drugs. Shortages of EML drugs are a policy failure, not just a supply chain event.
2. WHO reporting: member states are encouraged to report EML drug shortages to WHO through the medicines shortage reporting platform.
3. Price negotiations: EML status strengthens the hand of governments negotiating prices with manufacturers, particularly for patented or high-cost drugs like trastuzumab.
4. No guarantee of availability: EML inclusion is a normative statement, not an enforcement mechanism. Countries frequently experience shortages of EML-listed oncology drugs.

Policy implication for shortage risk briefs: When a drug on the WHO EML experiences a shortage in Argentina, Colombia, or Venezuela, it is not merely a supply chain problem — it is a failure of the health system's obligation to provide essential care. This framing is relevant to policy recommendations and the urgency assigned to shortage risk.
