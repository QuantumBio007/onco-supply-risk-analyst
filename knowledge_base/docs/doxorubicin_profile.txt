Doxorubicin is an anthracycline antibiotic chemotherapy agent used to treat breast cancer, leukemia, lymphoma, and sarcomas. It is one of the most broadly used oncology drugs and is on the WHO Model List of Essential Medicines (EML, oncology section). Conventional doxorubicin is fully off-patent and generic; liposomal doxorubicin has fewer manufacturers and higher shortage risk. India is the dominant global supplier of doxorubicin API (Active Pharmaceutical Ingredient), with a smaller contribution from China. Latin American countries — including Argentina, Colombia, and Venezuela — have no domestic API manufacturing capacity and are fully import-dependent on India and China for doxorubicin.

Formulation and storage: Doxorubicin is available as injectable solution and powder for reconstitution. It requires refrigeration at 2–8°C and must be protected from light. The cold chain requirement means that logistics disruptions — port delays, customs holds, power outages — directly threaten product integrity in addition to supply availability.

Colombia-specific regulatory context: In Colombia, doxorubicin must have an active INVIMA (Instituto Nacional de Vigilancia de Medicamentos y Alimentos) sanitary registration before commercial distribution. INVIMA is Colombia's drug regulatory authority — the equivalent of Argentina's ANMAT. Without a valid INVIMA registration for a specific manufacturer, that manufacturer cannot legally supply doxorubicin to Colombian EPS or hospital networks. This regulatory requirement creates a vulnerability during supply disruptions: alternative suppliers without INVIMA registration cannot rapidly substitute. As of 2024, INVIMA maintained a backlog of over 14,000 pending registrations across all product categories.

Currency devaluation impact: Doxorubicin is priced in USD in international trade. In Argentina, hospital procurement budgets are denominated in Argentine pesos. A currency devaluation directly reduces the purchasing power of hospitals and obras sociales buying imported doxorubicin, leading to reduced order volumes, deferred purchases, and increased stockout risk. This dynamic is particularly acute in public hospitals with fixed peso budgets.

Shortage history: Doxorubicin has experienced multiple documented global shortages. In 2011–2012, widespread shortages were reported in the United States due to manufacturing quality issues at major Indian and Chinese API producers. Subsequent shortages occurred in 2022–2023, again linked to API supply concentration among Indian manufacturers and manufacturing consolidation. These global shortages cascade into Latin American markets because the same Indian and Chinese API suppliers serve both developed-market and Latin American formulators. India is the primary global source of doxorubicin API; China is the secondary source. Argentina, Colombia, and Venezuela each rely entirely on Indian and Chinese API imports.
